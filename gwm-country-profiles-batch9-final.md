## Overview

Andorra is a tiny landlocked principality in the Pyrenees between France and Spain, with a population of approximately 77,000. Approximately 90% of Andorrans identify as Catholic. Christians practice entirely freely; there is no persecution. Andorra is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

COUNTRY: Australia
TITLE: Australia — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/australia
META DESCRIPTION: Australia is a majority-Christian nation with full religious freedom. The church’s role in Indigenous Australian history carries deep wounds, and the Catholic clergy abuse crisis produced a major royal commission. Christians practice freely while navigating a rapidly secularizing society.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Australia, Oceania, Catholic, Anglican, Evangelical, Religious Freedom, Indigenous Christianity, Abuse Scandal, Secularization
PERSECUTION TIER: Low

## Overview

Australia is approximately 44% Christian — a figure that has dropped significantly from 74% in 1991, reflecting one of the steeper secularizations in the Western world — with Anglican, Catholic, Uniting Church, and evangelical and Pentecostal communities all present. Christians practice entirely freely under full constitutional protection. Australia is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

## Christianity in Australia

Christianity arrived with British colonization in 1788. The Church of England was effectively established through the colonial period. Catholic missionaries followed, primarily serving Irish convict and immigrant communities. Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity has grown substantially since the late 20th century; Hillsong Church — founded in Sydney in 1983 — became one of the most globally influential evangelical worship movements of the 21st century.

## Indigenous Christianity and Colonial Wounds

The relationship between Christianity and Australia’s Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples is complex and painful. Missionaries were often among those who participated in the removal of Indigenous children from their families — the “Stolen Generations” — a policy that caused generational trauma and is a source of ongoing national reckoning. At the same time, Indigenous Australian Christianity is deeply rooted in many communities. The Uniting Church in Australia has been a significant institutional voice for Indigenous rights.

## The Royal Commission into Institutional Child Sexual Abuse

Australia’s Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse (2013–2017) was one of the most comprehensive investigations of church-related abuse in any country. It received over 8,000 personal accounts of abuse and examined the response of institutions including the Catholic Church, Anglican Church, and others. The Commission made 409 recommendations. The findings significantly accelerated secularization and fundamentally changed the Catholic Church’s approach to child protection in Australia.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~44% (declining from 74% in 1991)
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Notable:** Hillsong Church (1983, Sydney) — globally influential worship movement; Royal Commission into Institutional Child Sexual Abuse (2013–2017)

## Overview

Austria is approximately 57% Roman Catholic — though active practice is declining — with Lutheran, evangelical, and Orthodox minorities. The country has full constitutional religious freedom; Christians face no persecution. Austria is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

Austria’s Christian history is inseparable from the history of the Habsburg dynasty, which ruled the Holy Roman Empire and its successor states for centuries and positioned itself as the defender of Catholic Christianity in Central Europe. Vienna was besieged by Ottoman forces twice — in 1529 and again in 1683 — and the successful defense of 1683 (in which Polish King Jan III Sobieski’s forces were decisive) is celebrated in Catholic tradition as a providential deliverance. The composers Haydn, Mozart, Schubert, and Bruckner all wrote profoundly within the Catholic sacred music tradition.

- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution

COUNTRY: Kosovo
TITLE: Kosovo — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/kosovo
META DESCRIPTION: Kosovo declared independence from Serbia in 2008 and is majority Muslim. Its Serbian Orthodox Christian minority maintains ancient churches and monasteries — including UNESCO-listed medieval sites — in a context of post-conflict tension and ongoing concerns about religious heritage protection.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Kosovo, Balkans, Serbian Orthodox, Islam, Post-Conflict, Religious Heritage, Church Protection
PERSECUTION TIER: Moderate

## Overview

Kosovo is a partially recognized state in the Western Balkans — declared independent from Serbia in 2008 and recognized by over 100 countries, though not by Serbia, Russia, China, or five EU member states. Its population of approximately 1.8 million is approximately 95% Muslim (predominantly Albanian) and approximately 3–4% Serbian Orthodox Christian. Kosovo’s small Serbian Orthodox community maintains some of the most extraordinary medieval Christian heritage sites in the Balkans — including the UNESCO World Heritage-listed monasteries of Dečani, the Patriarchate of Peć, and the Church of the Holy Virgin of Ljeviša.

## The 1998–1999 War and Its Christian Dimension

The Kosovo War (1998–1999) between Serbian security forces and the Kosovo Liberation Army (KLA) resulted in NATO military intervention, the withdrawal of Serbian forces, and the mass displacement of Kosovo Albanians. In the aftermath, the situation reversed: hundreds of thousands of Kosovo Serbs fled or were expelled; Serbian Orthodox churches and monasteries were attacked; more than 150 churches and religious sites were damaged or destroyed between 1999 and 2004. The March 2004 riots — the worst anti-Serb violence since 1999 — targeted Serbian Orthodox sites specifically alongside Serb homes and villages.

## Current Status and Heritage Protection

Kosovo Serbs who remain live primarily in enclaves in northern Kosovo and in communities adjacent to significant monasteries. The monasteries — particularly Visoki Dečani, which maintains a community of monks — continue to function under KFOR (NATO-led peacekeeping force) protection. The monks of Dečani have been advocates for reconciliation and documented numerous threats and incidents. Vandalism and occasional attacks on Orthodox churches in Kosovo continue to be reported.

Kosovo is not on the Open Doors World Watch List for systematic persecution of Christians, but the situation of the Serbian Orthodox minority and the protection of ancient Christian heritage sites is a legitimate and documented concern.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~3–4% (Serbian Orthodox)
- **Primary Concerns:** Protection of Serbian Orthodox churches and monasteries; attacks on heritage sites; Serbian Orthodox minority living in enclaves with limited freedom of movement
- **Current Status:** Ancient Christian heritage sites require international protection; Serbian Orthodox community significantly reduced since 1999
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked

TITLE: Kuwait — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/kuwait
META DESCRIPTION: Kuwait is a Gulf Arab emirate where expatriate Christians can worship privately in designated areas. No Kuwaiti national can legally convert from Islam. Proselytism is criminal. The church exists for foreign workers only.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Kuwait, Gulf States, Islam, Expat Christians, Proselytism Ban, Converts, Religious Restrictions
PERSECUTION TIER: Moderate

## Overview

Kuwait is a constitutional monarchy on the Arabian Gulf with a population of approximately 4.3 million — of whom approximately 1.3 million are Kuwaiti nationals and nearly 3 million are expatriate workers. Islam is the state religion; the constitution requires that all legislation conform to Islamic Sharia principles. Christians represent approximately 14% of Kuwait’s total population, almost entirely among the expatriate community — Filipino, Indian, South Asian, and Western workers. Expatriate Christians can worship in designated church buildings; the Christian community operates legally within defined limits. But no Kuwaiti Muslim can convert to Christianity without facing devastating social and legal consequences, and proselytism directed at Muslims is a criminal offense.

## Church Buildings and Expatriate Worship

Kuwait has a small number of officially recognized church buildings serving the expatriate Christian community — Catholic, Anglican, Evangelical, Coptic, and several other denominations have established premises approved by the government. These churches serve the large expat population and operate under government oversight. Christians living in Kuwait can attend services, maintain pastoral care, and practice their faith privately.

## Restrictions on Kuwaiti Nationals

The laws governing Kuwaiti nationals are strict. A Kuwaiti who converts from Islam to Christianity faces loss of family ties under Islamic family law, potential disinheritance, social exclusion, and in some documented cases physical danger from family members. Proselytism to Kuwaiti nationals is illegal.

## Current Status

Kuwait is monitored in the moderate tier. By Gulf standards, it is comparable to Bahrain and Oman in terms of the framework offered to expatriate Christians. Kuwait is not on the Open Doors World Watch List’s top tiers.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~14% (almost entirely expatriate)
- **Primary Restrictions:** Proselytism criminal; Kuwaiti converts face severe consequences; worship limited to approved facilities
- **Current Status:** Expatriate worship tolerated in designated buildings; Kuwaiti nationals effectively barred from Christian faith
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not in top tier; monitored

COUNTRY: Kyrgyzstan
TITLE: Kyrgyzstan — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/kyrgyzstan
META DESCRIPTION: Kyrgyzstan is a Central Asian state where Christianity operates under mandatory registration requirements and growing Islamic pressure. Ethnic Kyrgyz converts face family and community hostility. The church is small, growing, and legally constrained.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Kyrgyzstan, Central Asia, Islam, Post-Soviet, Converts, Registration Restrictions, House Churches, WWL
PERSECUTION TIER: Moderate

## Overview

Kyrgyzstan is a mountainous Central Asian nation of approximately 7 million people, approximately 90% Muslim (predominantly Sunni). Christians represent approximately 7% of the population — predominantly ethnic Russians and Germans who maintained Christian identity through the Soviet period, with a growing community of ethnic Kyrgyz converts who came to faith primarily after 1991. Kyrgyzstan operates a state control framework for religious life similar to Kazakhstan and Tajikistan: mandatory registration, restricted evangelism, oversight of religious literature, and limits on religious education of minors. Ethnic Kyrgyz converts face family rejection and community ostracism in addition to legal risk.

## Historical Background

Russia’s imperial expansion into Central Asia in the 19th century brought Russian Orthodox Christianity into the region along with Russian settler communities. Soviet rule suppressed all religious life but Russian Orthodoxy survived in diminished form among ethnic Russian and German communities. Kyrgyzstan’s independence in 1991 opened the country to new Christian missions; South Korean and Western missionaries entered the country and established evangelical and Pentecostal congregations, with particular success among ethnic Kyrgyz who were open to something new after Soviet-imposed atheism.

## Government Control of Religion

Kyrgyzstan’s Religion Law requires all religious organizations to register with the State Commission on Religious Affairs. Unregistered religious activity is technically illegal. Unlike some neighboring states, Kyrgyzstan has not pursued systematic mass repression of unregistered churches — enforcement is inconsistent. However, foreign missionaries have been deported; some house church leaders have been arrested and fined; and the overall framework creates real constraints. The distribution of Christian literature requires state approval.

## Ethnic Kyrgyz Converts

The most vulnerable Christian community is the growing number of ethnic Kyrgyz converts. Their conversion is perceived culturally as a betrayal of Kyrgyz national identity — “Kyrgyz are Muslim” is a common formulation. Families may reject converts, arrange forced marriages to bring them back to Islamic practice, or in some documented cases use physical violence. Pastors who disciple Kyrgyz converts face threats.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~7%
- **Primary Threats:** Registration requirements for churches, social and family persecution of ethnic Kyrgyz converts, restrictions on religious literature and education, deportation of foreign missionaries
- **Current Status:** Legal framework constraining; enforcement inconsistent; converts most vulnerable
- **Open Doors WWL:** Monitored; moderate tier

## Overview

Uzbekistan is Central Asia’s most populous nation — approximately 36 million people — governed by a secular authoritarian system that exercises pervasive control over religious life. Approximately 88% of the population is Muslim; Christians represent approximately 8%, primarily ethnic Russians and Ukrainians belonging to the Russian Orthodox Church, with a small but growing community of evangelical and Pentecostal converts. The government controls all religious activity through mandatory registration requirements, surveillance, restrictions on religious literature, and bans on religious education of minors outside state-approved channels. Protestant and evangelical congregations face the most consistent pressure: raids on unregistered prayer meetings, fines for participants, confiscation of Bibles, and imprisonment of church leaders. Uzbekistan sits in the Open Doors World Watch List moderate tier.

## Government Religious Control

Uzbekistan’s Religion Law requires all religious organizations to register with the State Committee for Religious Affairs. The registration threshold — 100 founding members — is among the highest in Central Asia, effectively banning most small evangelical and Protestant congregations. Unregistered religious activity is illegal and subject to substantial fines and criminal charges. The distribution of religious literature requires state approval; unapproved Bibles and Christian materials are confiscated.

## Protestant Christians Under Pressure

Baptist, Pentecostal, and independent evangelical congregations are disproportionately targeted. Forum 18 News Service has documented dozens of raids on house church meetings, fines levied on participants, and imprisonment of church leaders for conducting “illegal religious activity.” Some evangelical leaders have been imprisoned for multi-year sentences. Uzbek converts from Islam face family rejection and social exclusion in addition to legal vulnerability.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~8%
- **Primary Threats:** Registration requirements (100-person threshold), raids on unregistered churches, fines and imprisonment, confiscation of religious literature, restrictions on religious education
- **Open Doors WWL:** Moderate tier ranked
- **Legal Status:** Christianity legal within registered frameworks; unregistered evangelical activity criminal
