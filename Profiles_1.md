COUNTRY: Afghanistan
TITLE: Afghanistan — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/afghanistan
META DESCRIPTION: Afghanistan is one of the world’s most dangerous countries for Christians. Under Taliban rule since 2021, all Christian practice is secret and apostasy carries a death sentence.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Afghanistan, Central Asia, Taliban, Islamic Extremism, WWL Top 10, Apostasy, Secret Believers
PERSECUTION TIER: High
WWL RANKING: #3 (Open Doors 2024)

## Overview

Afghanistan ranks among the world’s most dangerous nations for Christians. Under Taliban rule since August 2021, the country operates as a strict Islamic Emirate where apostasy and conversion from Islam carry potentially fatal consequences. The estimated Christian population — believed to number between 10,000 and 15,000 — practices in absolute secrecy. There are no public churches, no Christian institutions, and no legal pathway for a Muslim to become a Christian without risking their life. Afghanistan is a country where the church is not merely restricted. It is, as far as the outside world can see, invisible.

## Historical Roots of Christianity

The land now called Afghanistan was crossed by major Silk Road trade routes long before Islam arrived. Nestorian Christians — members of the Church of the East — established communities along these routes as early as the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD. Archaeological evidence suggests Christian presence in ancient Herat, Bagram, and the broader Khorasan region. By the 5th and 6th centuries, there is evidence of organized Nestorian church life under Sassanid Persian influence, including bishops and local communities.

These communities were not large, but they were real. Christianity had a foothold in the region for several centuries. The Arab Islamic conquest of the 7th century began the slow erasure of that presence. Over the following two hundred years, successive waves of Islamic governance, conversion pressure, and social transformation overwhelmed the Christian communities. By the 10th century, Nestorian Christianity had effectively ceased to exist in the region. The Mongol invasions of the 13th century further accelerated the consolidation of Islam as the region’s exclusive religious identity. By the Timurid period in the 15th century, there was no organized Christian community anywhere in what would become Afghanistan.

Western missionaries made limited contact during the 19th century British colonial era, but proselytism to Muslims was never seriously attempted. Afghanistan entered the 20th century as one of the most thoroughly and uniformly Islamic societies on earth.

## The Soviet Era and Civil War (1979–2001)

The Soviet invasion in December 1979 brought a decade of catastrophic violence followed by civil war. During this period, small numbers of foreign aid workers — many of them Christian — worked in Afghanistan under strict agreements to avoid any religious activity with Afghans. The mujahideen forces that drove out the Soviets were deeply Islamist, and any Afghan Christian during this era lived in total concealment.

The Taliban’s first period of control (1996–2001) formalized what had already been true for centuries: Afghanistan would tolerate no Christianity among its population. Foreign relief workers continued to operate at significant risk; several were arrested on charges of proselytizing. The legal framework was unambiguous — apostasy meant death, and the Taliban made clear they intended to enforce it.

## The Post-9/11 Decade (2001–2021)

Following the U.S.-led invasion in October 2001, a narrow window appeared. Western NGOs and relief organizations operated more freely, and some Afghan Christians — mostly converts who had come to faith outside the country — returned. Small house church networks developed, primarily in Kabul. The Afghan constitution of 2004 nominally guaranteed religious freedom for non-Muslims, but it simultaneously required all laws to conform to Islamic principles, and apostasy remained a capital offense in the prevailing legal and social understanding.

A number of high-profile apostasy cases emerged during this period. Abdul Rahman, who had converted to Christianity while working for an international aid organization, was arrested in 2006 and charged with apostasy. International pressure led to his release and emigration, but the case confirmed that Afghan law would not protect converts. Several South Korean missionaries were taken hostage by the Taliban in 2007; two were killed. The message was consistent: Afghanistan under any government would not tolerate Christian activity directed at Afghans.

## Taliban Return and Current Crisis (2021–Present)

The Taliban’s return in August 2021 ended the fragile openings of the previous two decades. Within weeks, known Christians fled or went into complete hiding. Underground church networks in Kabul went dark. Christian converts with foreign passports were evacuated by international organizations; those without escape routes were stranded and in acute danger.

Advocacy organizations including Open Doors, International Christian Concern, and Voice of the Martyrs describe a situation of extreme danger. Christians discovered by the Taliban face execution. Family members who report converts to authorities can be rewarded. The Taliban conducts door-to-door searches in some areas specifically seeking converts. Social media is monitored for any indication of Christian affiliation.

Afghanistan ranked #3 on the Open Doors World Watch List in 2024, placing it among the three most dangerous countries on earth for Christians. The Taliban interprets sharia as requiring the death penalty for apostasy. There is no legal mechanism for complaint, no international oversight of religious freedom inside Afghanistan, and no public church anywhere in the country.

## Afghan Christians in the Diaspora and Refugee System

Afghan Christians who flee to neighboring countries face additional hardship. Refugee camps in Pakistan and Iran are dominated by Muslim populations, and Christian converts face harassment or violence from fellow Afghans even outside their homeland. In Pakistan, Afghan Christian refugees have been attacked in camps and have faced deportation.

Significant numbers of Afghan Christians have settled in Western countries — Germany, the United Kingdom, the United States, Australia — following the 2021 collapse. These diaspora communities maintain contact with believers inside Afghanistan, support underground networks financially, and advocate internationally.

## The Church Today

The Afghan church is entirely underground and entirely invisible. Estimates of 10,000–15,000 believers are considered credible by Open Doors and similar organizations, though by definition there is no way to verify them. Most believers meet in groups of two or three. Larger gatherings are considered too dangerous. Communication happens through encrypted messaging applications. Audio Bibles in Dari and Pashto are distributed through secure channels by organizations that maintain contact with the underground community.

There are no seminaries, no church buildings, no Christian radio stations broadcasting legally inside the country, and no Christian schools. International advocates continue to document cases of imprisonment, torture, and execution, though precise numbers are impossible to verify.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #3
- **Estimated Christian Population:** 10,000–15,000 (all secret believers)
- **Primary Threat:** Taliban governance, apostasy laws, Islamic extremism
- **Legal Status:** No public worship permitted; apostasy a capital offense
- **Churches:** Zero public churches exist anywhere in the country
- **Bible:** Import illegal; possession dangerous
- **Foreign Missionaries:** Completely banned

COUNTRY: Albania
TITLE: Albania — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/albania
META DESCRIPTION: Albania was declared the world’s first atheist state in 1967. Today it enjoys religious freedom, but its Christian community carries the scars of decades of brutal communist suppression.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Albania, Southeast Europe, Communism, Religious Freedom, Eastern Orthodox, Balkans
PERSECUTION TIER: Moderate

## Overview

Albania occupies a distinctive place in Christian history: it was, for over two decades, the world’s only officially atheist state. The Hoxha regime banned all religion in 1967, demolished or repurposed churches and mosques alike, and imprisoned or executed clergy and believers. Today, Albania is legally free and religiously tolerant — but the legacy of that period still shapes the church. Recovery has been slow, communities are fragmented, and the country remains one of the least-Christianized in Europe even where legal barriers no longer exist.

## Historical Roots of Christianity

Christianity arrived in the region the Romans called Illyricum very early. The Apostle Paul references preaching as far as Illyricum in Romans 15:19, and tradition holds that the faith reached what is now Albanian territory in the first century AD. Organized church structures developed during the Byzantine period. The Albanian church was divided between Eastern Orthodoxy in the south and Roman Catholicism in the north — a split that roughly corresponds to geography and persists to this day.

Ottoman conquest in the 15th century brought Islam to Albania over the following centuries. By the 20th century, roughly 70% of Albanians identified as Muslim. The remainder were split between Orthodox and Catholic communities. Notably, Albanian national identity did not align neatly with any single religion — the independence movement of the 19th and early 20th centuries was explicitly multi-confessional, and figures like Pashko Vasa famously declared that the religion of Albanians was “Albanianism.”

## Communist Suppression (1967–1990)

Enver Hoxha declared Albania the world’s first atheist state in 1967. This was not merely rhetorical. All 2,169 religious buildings in the country — churches, mosques, monasteries, tekkes — were closed, demolished, or repurposed as sports halls, warehouses, or cultural centers. Religious practice of any kind was criminalized. Clergy were imprisoned; those who continued to practice their faith secretly were executed or sentenced to decades of forced labor.

Catholic clergy were particularly persecuted. Archbishop Vinçens Prennushi died in prison in 1949. Dozens of priests were executed in the late 1940s and early 1950s. The surviving Catholic Church went entirely underground. When the regime fell in 1990, almost no clergy remained in the country — most had died, fled, or survived in hiding.

## Post-Communist Recovery and Current Status

The fall of communism in 1990–1991 brought legal religious freedom, but rebuilding from essentially zero required decades. The Orthodox Church of Albania was reestablished under Archbishop Anastasios Yannoulatos, a Greek scholar who arrived in 1992 and led a significant rebuilding effort. Protestant and evangelical missions also entered the country in the 1990s.

Today, Albania has formal religious freedom. The constitution guarantees it; the government generally respects it. The rough breakdown is approximately 57% Muslim, 17% Orthodox, 10% Catholic, and the remainder irreligious or other. Interreligious relations are historically cooperative — Albanians point with pride to their tradition of sheltering Jews during the Holocaust and to multi-confessional nationalism.

Albania is not a persecution country by any standard measure. The legacy of Hoxha’s atheism is not ongoing repression but rather a society still rebuilding its religious memory — a country where the church was nearly killed and is slowly, imperfectly, coming back to life.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~27% (Orthodox and Catholic combined)
- **Primary Historical Threat:** Communist atheist suppression (1967–1990)
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no systematic persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Notable:** Albania sheltered Jews during the Holocaust regardless of religion — a unique record in occupied Europe

TITLE: Algeria — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/algeria
META DESCRIPTION: Algeria was once the heartland of North African Christianity. Today it is a nation of 99% Muslims where the church is growing quietly among Berber communities — and where the government is closing that church, building by building.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Algeria, North Africa, Islam, Berber, Kabyle, Church Closures, WWL Top 25, Apostasy
PERSECUTION TIER: High
WWL RANKING: #22 (Open Doors 2024)

## Overview

Algeria presents one of Christianity’s most striking reversals. The region now called Algeria was, in the early centuries of the church, one of its intellectual and spiritual powerhouses. Tertullian, Cyprian, and Augustine — three of the most formative theologians in Christian history — all came from North Africa. Today, Algeria is 99% Muslim, and the government has spent the last decade systematically closing the small Protestant churches that had begun to grow among the Kabyle Berber population. The story of Algeria is a story of erasure, recovery, and ongoing pressure.

## The Ancient Christian Heartland

North African Christianity was not a colonial import. It was a living, indigenous faith that shaped the entire church. Tertullian of Carthage (c. 155–220 AD) was among the first to write Christian theology in Latin, and his work shaped the Western church’s vocabulary for centuries. Cyprian, Bishop of Carthage (c. 200–258 AD), was martyred under Roman persecution. Most significantly, Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD) — born in Thagaste, in what is now northeastern Algeria — became arguably the single most influential theologian in Western Christian history. His Confessions and City of God shaped Catholic and Protestant thought alike for over a millennium.

The North African church was not simply scholarly. It was a martyr church. Perpetua and Felicity, martyred in Carthage in 203 AD, are among the most celebrated martyrs in Christian history. By the 4th century, North Africa had more Christian bishops than any other region in the Roman world.

## Islamic Conquest and the Disappearance of Christianity

The Arab Islamic conquest began in the 7th century. Unlike in Egypt, where the Coptic church survived Islamic rule and persists to this day, North African Christianity essentially disappeared. By the 12th century, organized Christianity had ceased to exist in the Maghreb. French colonization beginning in 1830 brought Catholic missionaries back to the region, establishing churches primarily for European settlers. When independence came in 1962, the European settler population — and most of the Catholic institutional presence — departed with it.

## The Kabyle Revival

Beginning in the 1990s, something unexpected happened. Among the Kabyle Berbers of the mountainous Kabylia region east of Algiers, a significant movement toward Christianity began. The Kabyle are ethnically and culturally distinct from Arab Algerians; they speak Tamazight and have historically maintained a distinct identity. The turn to Christianity among many Kabyle was partly a rejection of Arab-Islamic identity politics, and partly a response to genuine spiritual seeking. Evangelical and Protestant Christianity grew rapidly through informal networks, house churches, and radio ministry.

By the 2000s, estimates suggested tens of thousands of Kabyle Christians — a dramatic and unexpected development. The Protestant Church of Algeria (EPA) registered congregations and operated with a degree of visibility that would have been unthinkable a generation earlier.

## Government Response: Church Closures

The Algerian government’s response hardened significantly in the 2000s and has intensified since. Ordonnance 06-03, passed in 2006, restricts non-Muslim worship and criminalizes “inciting, constraining, or utilizing means of seduction tending to convert a Muslim to another religion.” Beginning around 2017, the government launched a systematic campaign of church closures in the Kabylia region and beyond. The EPA reported that more than 50 churches were closed between 2017 and 2020, with authorities citing administrative violations. By 2022, the number of closures had surpassed 100 congregations.

Pastor Salah Chalah, president of the EPA, faced multiple legal challenges. Algerian Christians who convert from Islam — though apostasy is not technically a criminal offense in the penal code — face intense social, family, and community pressure, and in some cases legal harassment on other pretexts.

## Current Status

Algeria’s Christian community is estimated at 50,000–100,000. Open Doors ranked Algeria at #22 on the 2024 World Watch List. The primary pressures are government-imposed restrictions, church closures, and the social consequences of conversion from Islam. The underground church continues — Algerians who have come to faith do not easily give it up — but the space for open Christian community is narrowing.

Algeria’s story is a reminder that the most ancient Christian territory on earth is not necessarily the most receptive to the gospel today — and that a church the state cannot destroy with violence, it may attempt to strangle with paperwork.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #22
- **Estimated Christian Population:** 50,000–100,000 (majority Kabyle Berber converts)
- **Primary Threats:** Government church closures, Ordinance 06-03, social pressure on converts
- **Legal Status:** Non-Muslim worship technically permitted in registered buildings; new construction effectively banned
- **Churches:** 100+ closed or sealed since 2017
- **Notable History:** Birthplace of Augustine, Tertullian, and Cyprian; once the intellectual center of Western Christianity

## Overview

Angola is among the most Christian nations in Africa. Roughly 90% of the population identifies as Christian, with the Catholic Church — introduced by Portuguese missionaries in the late 15th century — still the dominant institution and Protestant denominations growing rapidly. Angola is not a persecution country. However, the government exercises meaningful control over religious organizations, unregistered groups have faced restrictions, and the country’s history carries the weight of how Christianity was intertwined with colonial rule in complex and sometimes damaging ways.

## Historical Roots

Portuguese explorer Diogo Cão made contact with the Kingdom of Kongo in 1483, and Catholic missionaries followed almost immediately. The Kongolese king Nzinga a Ntinu was baptized as João I in 1491, making the Kingdom of Kongo one of the earliest African states to officially adopt Christianity. The subsequent relationship between the Kongolese monarchy and the Catholic Church was genuine — Kongolese nobles sent their sons to be educated in Portugal, and a Kongolese man, Henrique, was ordained a bishop in 1518, among the first indigenous African bishops in church history. Christianity was not merely imposed on Kongo; it was adopted, adapted, and made their own.

Portuguese colonial rule, which deepened over subsequent centuries, increasingly used the Catholic Church as an instrument of administration. When independence came in 1975 after a lengthy liberation war, the new MPLA government was Marxist and initially hostile to the church — nationalizing church schools and hospitals and restricting religious activity. However, unlike other Marxist states, Angola never fully suppressed the church.

## Post-War Recovery and Current Status

Angola’s devastating civil war (1975–2002) killed an estimated 500,000 people and displaced millions. Churches provided some of the only social services available in war-torn areas, which deepened the relationship between the population and the faith. Since the end of the war in 2002, Christianity has grown significantly.

The government has at times restricted new or unregistered religious movements. In 2018, Angola banned approximately 194 religious groups that were not officially recognized, requiring them to re-register under tighter criteria. The ban on Jehovah’s Witnesses was later lifted following legal challenges. These actions reflect a pattern of government oversight of religion rather than anti-Christian persecution — the same restrictions affected groups across religious lines.

Angola is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~90%
- **Primary Denominations:** Catholic (~60%), Protestant/Evangelical (~30%)
- **Current Status:** Religious freedom with some registration requirements; not a persecution country
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Historical Note:** Kingdom of Kongo was among Africa’s first Christian kingdoms (1491)

## Overview

Argentina is one of the most Christian countries in the Western Hemisphere. Approximately 92% of the population identifies as Christian, with the Roman Catholic Church historically dominant and evangelical Protestantism growing rapidly — particularly Pentecostal movements — over the past several decades. The country’s constitution guarantees full religious freedom, and Christians face no systematic persecution of any kind.

## Christianity in Argentina

Spanish colonization in the 16th century brought Catholicism as the dominant faith. The Jesuit missions in the northeast — the famous Reducciones — became models of organized Christian community among the indigenous Guaraní people and left a lasting architectural and cultural legacy. Argentina gained independence in 1816, and while the Catholic Church retained enormous social influence throughout the 19th and most of the 20th century, Protestant denominations also took root.

Since the 1980s, evangelical and Pentecostal churches have grown dramatically. Pope Francis — born Jorge Mario Bergoglio in Buenos Aires — was elected to lead the Catholic Church in 2013, a source of national pride that reinforced Argentina’s deep Catholic identity.

## Current Status

Argentina’s constitution formally recognizes Catholicism as the official religion, though this has been interpreted more as a statement of cultural heritage than as an establishment of religion in the restrictive sense. All religious groups are free to operate. There is no persecution, no meaningful restriction on worship or evangelism, and no pattern of violence against Christians as Christians.

SLUG: country-profiles/armenia

CATEGORY: Country Profiles

## Overview

Armenia holds a singular place in Christian history: it was the first nation in the world to adopt Christianity as its official state religion. In 301 AD, King Tiridates III was baptized and declared Christianity Armenia’s state faith — preceding the Roman Empire’s Edict of Milan by twelve years. That declaration set the terms of Armenian identity for seventeen centuries. To be Armenian has long been inseparable from being Christian. That identity has been tested by Persian invasions, Arab conquest, Ottoman oppression, and Soviet atheism — and it has survived all of them, though at enormous cost.

## The First Christian Nation

According to tradition, the Apostles Thaddaeus and Bartholomew brought Christianity to Armenia in the 1st century AD. Gregory the Illuminator — imprisoned for thirteen years by Tiridates III before the king converted — became the founder of the Armenian Apostolic Church and is venerated as its patron saint. The church Gregory established is one of the oldest national churches in the world and has maintained an unbroken institutional presence for seventeen centuries.

The Armenian Apostolic Church developed independently of both Rome and Constantinople. It rejected the Council of Chalcedon in 451 AD, placing it within the Oriental Orthodox communion alongside the Coptic, Ethiopian, and Syriac churches. The creation of the Armenian alphabet by Mesrop Mashtots in 405 AD was directly tied to the church — developed specifically to translate the Bible and liturgical texts, it gave Armenians a literary culture rooted in their faith and protected them from assimilation into Persian, Greek, or Arabic cultural worlds.

## Centuries of Persecution

The Battle of Avarayr in 451 AD, in which Armenian Christian forces fought the Sassanid Persian Empire to defend the right to practice Christianity, is remembered as a defining moment: the Armenians lost the battle but negotiated a peace that preserved their faith. Vartan Mamikonian, who led the Christian forces, is venerated as a saint.

Under Arab rule in the 7th–9th centuries, Armenian Christians were taxed as dhimmis but generally permitted to practice their faith. The Seljuk Turk invasions of the 11th century caused massive displacement and the destruction of many historic churches. Under the Ottoman Empire, Armenians lived as a recognized millet with limited self-governance but were subject to periodic massacres and systematic discrimination.

The worst came in the 20th century. The Armenian Genocide of 1915–1923, carried out by the Ottoman Empire and the subsequent Turkish nationalist government, killed an estimated 600,000 to 1.5 million Armenians. Christian identity was not incidental to the genocide — Armenians were targeted explicitly as Christians in what the perpetrators framed partly as a religious and civilizational conflict. The genocide scattered the Armenian diaspora across the globe and left deep wounds that have not healed.

## Soviet Period and Independence

Soviet rule (1920–1991) brought state atheism and suppression of the Armenian Apostolic Church. Church properties were confiscated, clergy were monitored and sometimes imprisoned, and public religious practice was restricted. However, the Soviets could not fully suppress the Armenian church — its identification with national identity made it too deeply rooted. The church survived, diminished but intact.

Armenia declared independence in 1991. Christianity was immediately reaffirmed as central to national identity. The Armenian Apostolic Church was restored to its prominent social and cultural role.

## Current Status and Tensions

Armenia today has legal religious freedom. The Armenian Apostolic Church holds a privileged status — it is referenced in the constitution as the national church — but this does not amount to systematic persecution of other Christians. Some minority religious groups — particularly Jehovah’s Witnesses — have historically faced difficulties, though the situation has improved since the early 2000s.

The September 2023 Azerbaijani military offensive led to the displacement of virtually the entire Armenian Christian population of Nagorno-Karabakh — over 100,000 people. The fate of those historic churches and cemeteries under Azerbaijani control remains a concern.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~97% (predominantly Armenian Apostolic)
- **Current Status:** Religious freedom; privileged status for Armenian Apostolic Church; minority denominations generally free
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Historical Note:** First nation to adopt Christianity as state religion (301 AD); 1915 Genocide killed up to 1.5 million Armenian Christians

META DESCRIPTION: Azerbaijan is a secular Shia-majority state with strict government control over all religious life. Christians face registration barriers, raids, and surveillance. The displacement of 100,000 Armenian Christians from Nagorno-Karabakh in 2023 marked a major regional crisis.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Azerbaijan, Caucasus, Islam, Shia, Religious Restrictions, Nagorno-Karabakh, Armenian Christians, WWL
PERSECUTION TIER: Moderate

## Overview

Azerbaijan presents itself as a modern, secular state — and in some respects it is. But the government exercises pervasive control over religious life, and Christian communities operate in an environment of registration requirements, surveillance, raids, and deportation of foreign missionaries. The country is approximately 97% Muslim (predominantly Shia, reflecting historical Persian influence), with a small Christian minority including Russian Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, and various Protestant communities. The September 2023 military operation that ended Armenian Christian presence in Nagorno-Karabakh represents a significant humanitarian and religious crisis connected to Azerbaijan’s actions.

## Historical Roots of Christianity

Christianity arrived in the Caucasus early. The ancient kingdom of Caucasian Albania (not related to the Balkan nation) occupied much of what is now Azerbaijan and converted to Christianity in the 4th century. Apostolic tradition credits Bartholomew and Thaddaeus with bringing the faith to the region. Persia’s long dominance and the Safavid dynasty’s 16th-century conversion of the population to Shia Islam gradually reduced Christianity to a small minority presence.

## Government Control of Religion

Since independence in 1991, Azerbaijan’s government has maintained tight control over religious organizations through the State Committee on Work with Religious Organizations. All religious communities must register with this body. Unregistered religious activity is illegal and subject to fines, closure, and arrest.

A 2011 law on religious organizations tightened restrictions further, banning religious education of minors outside officially approved settings and requiring all religious literature to be approved by the state before distribution. For Christians, registration requirements create significant barriers. House churches and unregistered Protestant congregations have faced raids. Foreign missionaries have been detained and deported.

## The Nagorno-Karabakh Crisis

Nagorno-Karabakh — a mountainous enclave historically populated predominantly by ethnic Armenians and home to dozens of ancient Armenian Christian churches and monasteries — was the subject of conflict between Armenia and Azerbaijan since the late Soviet period. In September 2023, a brief Azerbaijani military offensive led to the flight of virtually the entire Armenian Christian population — over 100,000 people — to Armenia within days. The displacement has raised significant concerns about the fate of ancient Christian heritage sites now under Azerbaijani control.

## Current Status

Azerbaijan is included in the Open Doors World Watch List, reflecting meaningful restrictions on Christian practice. Physical violence against Christians is rare. The primary tools of pressure are legal and administrative: registration requirements, restrictions on religious literature, surveillance, fines, and deportation of foreign Christian workers.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~3% (Russian Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Protestant)
- **Primary Threats:** Government registration requirements, raids, deportation of missionaries, surveillance
- **Current Status:** Restricted but not violently persecuted; legal framework is significantly constraining
- **Open Doors WWL:** Ranked
- **Critical Event:** 100,000+ Armenian Christians displaced from Nagorno-Karabakh in September 2023

## Overview

Bangladesh is the world’s eighth-most-populous country and one of its most densely inhabited. Islam is the state religion — enshrined in the constitution since 1988 — and approximately 91% of the population is Muslim. Christians constitute roughly 0.4% of the population, numbering around 700,000 people. They face a compound set of pressures: Islamist extremist violence targeting pastors and churches, severe social and family persecution directed at converts from Islam, discrimination in employment and education, and the steady growth of political Islamism that constrains the space available for minority religious life. Bangladesh ranked #31 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List.

## Historical Roots of Christianity

Christianity arrived in Bengal primarily during the British colonial period. Catholic missionaries — particularly Portuguese Augustinians — were present in the region as early as the 16th century. British Protestant missionaries arrived in the 19th century; the Baptist Missionary Society, Methodists, and others established churches, schools, and hospitals. The Christianity that took deepest root was not primarily among the Bengali-speaking Muslim majority, but among the indigenous tribal communities of the Chittagong Hill Tracts — the Chakma, Marma, Tripura, and other groups, many of whom converted in large numbers.

## The Creation of Bangladesh and Early Decades

Bangladesh was created in 1971 through a violent liberation war against Pakistani rule. The Pakistani army’s campaign of atrocities — which killed an estimated 300,000 to 3,000,000 people — also affected Christian communities. The independent Bangladesh that emerged in 1971 was initially secular in its constitutional framework. This changed dramatically after 1975 as successive military governments moved the country in a more Islamist direction. The 1988 constitutional amendment declaring Islam the state religion was a milestone in that shift.

## Islamist Extremism and Violence

The period 2015–2016 was particularly devastating. A wave of targeted assassinations — carried out with machetes by jihadist cells — killed or attempted to kill secular bloggers, Hindu priests, Christian pastors, LGBT activists, and foreigners. These attacks were claimed by groups affiliated with both al-Qaeda’s South Asian affiliate (AQIS) and the Islamic State. In July 2016, the Holey Artisan Bakery attack in Dhaka — in which 22 hostages were killed after gunmen separated victims by whether they could recite Quranic verses — shocked the international community and demonstrated the reach of Islamist violence in Bangladesh’s capital.

Pastors have been attacked in their homes and on the streets. Churches have been vandalized and in some cases bombed. In the Chittagong Hill Tracts, indigenous Christian communities face violence not only from Islamist groups but from Bengali settlers who encroach on their land with government support or tolerance, creating a compound persecution combining religious and ethnic dimensions.

The Bangladeshi government prosecuted some Islamist militant cells, particularly after the 2016 Holey Artisan attack prompted a sustained security crackdown. However, the underlying social and ideological conditions that generate extremism have not been fully addressed.

## Social Persecution and Converts

A Muslim who converts to Christianity faces the loss of family relationships, inheritance rights, and social standing. In rural communities, converts may be driven from their homes, have their property seized, or face community-organized pressure including violence. Pastors who baptize converts from Muslim backgrounds face threats from the convert’s family and sometimes from local authorities.

Employment discrimination is widespread. Christians report being passed over for government positions, facing hostility from Muslim colleagues, and encountering informal exclusion from business networks. In educational settings, Christian children sometimes face pressure to participate in Islamic religious instruction.

## Chittagong Hill Tracts: A Separate Crisis

The indigenous Christians of the Chittagong Hill Tracts face a distinct set of pressures. Bengali settlers, encouraged by the government, have moved into the Tracts, displacing indigenous communities and seizing land. The military maintains a heavy presence. Land grabs and physical violence against indigenous communities — including Christian ones — are documented by human rights organizations.

## Current Status

Open Doors ranked Bangladesh at #31 on the 2024 World Watch List. The current political situation — following the political upheaval of 2024 that removed Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina — introduced uncertainty about whether trends in religious freedom would improve or worsen.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #31

- **Primary Threats:** Islamist extremism, violence against pastors, social persecution of converts, Chittagong Hill Tracts land seizure and violence
- **Legal Status:** Christianity is legal; social enforcement of apostasy is severe

## Overview

Belarus is predominantly Eastern Orthodox Christian — roughly 48% identify as Orthodox — with a significant Catholic minority and a variety of Protestant denominations. Under President Alexander Lukashenko, who has ruled since 1994, religious life is subject to state control. The Orthodox majority worships largely without interference. Minority denominations — particularly Protestants — navigate restrictive registration requirements, periodic harassment, and a chilling effect in a state that regards independent civil society organizations, including religious ones, with suspicion.

## Christianity in Belarus

Christianity arrived in the region with the Christianization of Kievan Rus in 988 AD. The Belarusian lands were part of subsequent Eastern Orthodox development, though under the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, Catholic influence was also significant, leading to the creation of the Greek Catholic (Uniate) Church in the 1596 Union of Brest. The Soviet period brought atheist suppression across all denominations. After independence in 1991, Belarus saw a religious revival.

## Lukashenko’s Religious Policy

A 2002 Law on Religion significantly restricted religious activity. It requires all religious organizations to re-register with the government, mandates a minimum membership of 20 adults for registration, prohibits unregistered religious activity, and gives authorities broad discretion to refuse or cancel registration. Protestant groups — Baptists, Pentecostals, Adventists, evangelical congregations — have faced recurring difficulties. Some have been denied registration and forced to operate illegally. The fraudulent August 2020 presidential election triggered massive popular protests. Several church leaders — including Catholic priests and Protestant pastors — publicly expressed support for protesters or provided sanctuary to those fleeing riot police. The government’s response included mass arrests, torture in detention, and a systematic campaign against civil society. Churches that had been vocal faced intensified scrutiny. At least one Catholic priest was expelled; Protestant pastors who had been visible in the protest movement faced pressure.

## Current Status

Belarus is not on the Open Doors World Watch List as a top-tier persecution country. Christians who belong to the recognized Orthodox and Catholic churches can worship largely freely. The restrictions fall most heavily on unregistered or minority Protestant communities and on any religious leader who engages in politically sensitive speech.

## Overview

Bhutan is a small, landlocked Himalayan kingdom — population approximately 800,000 — famous internationally for its Gross National Happiness philosophy and its stunning Buddhist cultural landscape. Drukpa Kagyu Buddhism is the state religion, woven into the constitutional fabric and administered by a powerful monastic establishment. Christianity has no legal recognition in Bhutan. No churches may be built. Foreign missionaries are banned. Conversion from Buddhism, while not explicitly criminalized in a standalone law, is treated as a social and civic offense that can result in dismissal from government employment, loss of access to public services, family estrangement, and in some documented cases, legal harassment on other pretexts. An estimated 10,000–20,000 Bhutanese Christians practice in secret house churches.

## Buddhism, Bhutan, and Religious Identity

Bhutan’s relationship with Buddhism is not incidental. The country’s founding mythology, its political structures, its art, its architecture, and its sense of national identity are all deeply bound to Drukpa Kagyu Buddhism. The Je Khenpo — the head of the monastic establishment — holds constitutional authority comparable in scope to a prime minister in religious and cultural matters. The concept of Gross National Happiness is explicitly grounded in Buddhist values of contentment, harmony, and spiritual balance.

## The History of Christianity in Bhutan

Christianity has no ancient roots in Bhutan. The country was deliberately isolated from outside influence for most of its history. Jesuit priests briefly visited the country in the 17th century but established no lasting presence. Modern Christianity in Bhutan began largely through Bhutanese who encountered the faith outside the country — in Indian cities, through contact with NGOs and aid organizations, or through study abroad.

## The Lhotshampa Expulsion

In the early 1990s, approximately 100,000 ethnic Nepali Bhutanese — called Lhotshampa — were expelled from the country after the government pursued a policy of cultural homogenization. They fled primarily to refugee camps in Nepal, where many converted to Christianity through missionary organizations working in the camps. When resettlement to third countries occurred from the mid-2000s onward, a large portion of the resettled Bhutanese were Christian. Bhutanese diaspora Christian communities are now active in advocacy for religious freedom in their homeland.

## The Church in Bhutan Today

Bhutanese Christians practice entirely in secret. House churches meet in small groups, typically fewer than ten people, in private homes. Christians in Bhutan face discrimination in civil service employment. Pastors and house church leaders who are identified face the risk of arrest, prosecution on charges related to disturbing public order or religious harmony, fines, and in some cases imprisonment. Bibles are not available in bookshops; they circulate through personal networks in physical copies or digitally through encrypted channels.

## Current Status

Open Doors ranked Bhutan at #38 on the 2024 World Watch List. The situation is not improving significantly. The constitutional framework that disadvantages Christians is stable, the monastic establishment has no interest in change, and the government lacks both the political incentive and the public pressure to reform.

- **Legal Status:** No explicit apostasy law but conversion treated as civic and social offense
- **Churches:** Zero public churches; all meetings in secret house groups
- **Foreign Missionaries:** Completely banned

## Overview

Bosnia and Herzegovina is a country where religious identity and ethnic identity are almost inseparable. The three “constituent peoples” recognized by the Dayton Agreement are defined primarily by their religious heritage: Bosniaks (Bosnian Muslims, ~51%), Serbs (Eastern Orthodox, ~31%), and Croats (Roman Catholic, ~15%). Christians are not a persecuted minority in Bosnia — they are part of the majority fabric — but the legacy of that war continues to shape interreligious relations in ways that affect how all communities live.

## Christianity in Bosnia: Historical Roots

Christianity arrived in the Balkan region during the early centuries of the church, with the region experiencing division between Rome and Constantinople. The medieval Bosnian church — the Crkva Bosanska — was a distinctive institution about which historians debate. Ottoman conquest began in 1463 and eventually brought Islam as a significant presence.

## The 1992–1995 War and Religious Destruction

Serbian nationalist forces, pursuing a campaign of ethnic cleansing, systematically targeted Bosniak Muslims and, to a lesser extent, Bosnian Croats. The destruction of religious sites was deliberate and extensive: mosques were demolished in areas under Serbian control; Catholic churches were destroyed in areas under Bosniak or Serbian control. The Srebrenica massacre of July 1995, in which approximately 8,000 Bosniak Muslim men and boys were killed by Bosnian Serb forces under General Ratko Mladic, is classified by the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia as genocide. Christians were perpetrators of significant violence in the war as well as victims — this complexity is important to acknowledge.

## Post-War Situation and Current Status

The Dayton Agreement of 1995 ended the war by dividing Bosnia into two entities. Today, Bosnia has formal religious freedom. Destroyed religious sites have been substantially rebuilt. Evangelical and other minority Protestant denominations operate in Bosnia with some bureaucratic friction but not systematic persecution. Bosnia is not on the Open Doors World Watch List as a persecution country for Christians.

The official state ideology — Melayu Islam Beraja (Malay Islamic Monarchy, or MIB) — enshrines Islam, Malay culture, and the sultanate as the three pillars of national identity. Christianity is practiced primarily by Chinese Bruneians and expatriate workers, representing approximately 10% of the population. Christians can worship privately and existing church buildings may be used, but they operate under a restrictive legal framework: no new churches may be built, Christmas celebrations outside the home are limited, Bibles and Christian materials cannot be imported for public distribution, proselytism directed at Muslims is illegal, and the 2019 implementation of the Sharia Penal Code introduced the death penalty for apostasy — though no executions under this code had been documented as of early 2025.

## Restrictions in Practice

The practical restrictions on Brunei’s Christians include: no new church buildings permitted for decades; Christmas celebrations outside private homes and approved church premises restricted; importation of Christian literature controlled; death penalty for apostasy enacted under the 2019 Sharia Penal Code Phase 3; and no evangelism to Muslims. The 2019 implementation attracted international condemnation. The government subsequently stated that the moratorium on capital punishment would continue.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~10% (primarily Chinese Bruneians and expatriates)
- **Primary Restrictions:** No new churches permitted; Christmas celebrations restricted; Sharia apostasy law enacted 2019; no evangelism to Muslims
- **Current Status:** Private worship permitted; significant legal and institutional restrictions
- **Open Doors WWL:** Ranked (mid-tier)
- **Notable:** 2019 Sharia Penal Code Phase 3 introduced death penalty for apostasy; international condemnation followed

## Overview

Bulgaria is a majority Eastern Orthodox Christian country — approximately 76% of the population identifies with the Bulgarian Orthodox Church. Religious freedom is constitutionally guaranteed, and Christians of all denominations worship without systematic persecution. Bulgaria is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

Bulgaria was Christianized in 864 AD under Khan Boris I, who adopted Eastern Christianity and aligned Bulgaria with the Byzantine ecclesiastical sphere. The creation of the Cyrillic alphabet — attributed to Saints Cyril and Methodius and developed by their disciples in Bulgaria — was directly tied to the Christianization project, enabling scripture and liturgy in the Slavic language.

The communist period (1944–1989) suppressed religious life, closed monasteries, arrested clergy, and promoted atheism. After 1989, the church recovered. Some newer religious movements — Jehovah’s Witnesses, Scientology — have encountered registration difficulties or social hostility, but not systematic state persecution.

COUNTRY: Burkina Faso
TITLE: Burkina Faso — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/burkina-faso
META DESCRIPTION: Burkina Faso has become one of the world’s most dangerous countries for Christians. Since 2015, jihadist militants have systematically targeted churches, beheaded pastors, and forced hundreds of thousands to flee. The situation is rapidly deteriorating.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Burkina Faso, West Africa, Islamic Extremism, Jihadism, JNIM, ISIS, WWL Top 25, Church Attacks, Sahel
PERSECUTION TIER: High
WWL RANKING: #22 (Open Doors 2024)

## Overview

Burkina Faso was once held up as a model of Muslim-Christian coexistence in West Africa. Since 2015, a jihadist insurgency — connected to al-Qaeda affiliate Jama’at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin (JNIM) and the Islamic State in the Greater Sahara (ISGS) — has transformed the country into one of the most dangerous places on earth to be a Christian. Churches have been burned by the hundreds. Pastors have been publicly beheaded. Entire Christian villages have been massacred. Hundreds of thousands of Christians have been internally displaced. In large portions of the country’s north and east, the church has effectively ceased to exist in visible form.

## Historical Background

Burkina Faso — known as Upper Volta until a 1984 revolutionary renaming — is a landlocked Sahelian nation in West Africa with a population of approximately 22 million. French colonization (from the late 19th century) brought Catholic missionaries, who established missions, schools, and hospitals across the country. Today, approximately 61% of Burkina Faso is Muslim, 24% Christian (Catholic and Protestant roughly equally), and 15% traditional animist. Historically, these communities coexisted with a degree of harmony unusual even by regional standards.

## The Jihadist Insurgency: Origins and Escalation

The insurgency began in northern Burkina Faso around 2015–2016, connected to armed groups originating in Mali. JNIM — affiliated with al-Qaeda — was the primary force in the north and west. ISGS operated primarily in the east. Both groups pursued the imposition of their interpretation of Islamic governance and the elimination of perceived enemies including Christians.

Christians were specifically and systematically targeted. The pattern was consistent: armed groups entered Christian villages, separated Christians from Muslims, demanded that Christians renounce their faith, and killed those who refused. Churches were burned — not as incidental collateral damage but as deliberate acts of religious erasure.

## Documented Attacks on Christians

Key incidents include: In April 2019, militants attacked Dabo, killing a Catholic priest and five worshipers during a church service. In 2019, more than 100 church buildings were attacked, vandalized, or destroyed. In June 2020, militants attacked Pansi village and demanded that residents renounce Christianity — those who refused were shot. On Easter Sunday 2023, approximately 44 people were massacred in Yateba, with Christians specifically targeted.

## The Humanitarian Crisis

An estimated 2 million people are internally displaced in Burkina Faso as of early 2025. A large proportion of the displaced are Christians from northern and eastern regions who fled when jihadist groups took control of their areas. In jihadist-controlled areas, Christians cannot worship, carry Bibles, or maintain any visible religious identity.

## Government Response

Burkina Faso has experienced two military coups in 2022. The successive military juntas expelled French forces and pivoted toward Russia (and the Wagner Group/Africa Corps) for security assistance. The military government’s capacity to protect civilians remains deeply limited.

## The Church Today

In the capital Ouagadougou and other urban centers, churches remain open and active. In rural areas north of the 13th parallel, organized church life has largely collapsed. Pastors either fled or were killed. Congregations dispersed. The Burkinabè diaspora maintains active prayer and advocacy networks internationally.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #22
- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~24% (~5.5 million)
- **Primary Threat:** Jihadist armed groups (JNIM, ISGS); deliberate targeting of churches, pastors, and Christian communities
- **Displacement:** Estimated 2 million internally displaced, large proportion Christian
- **Churches Destroyed:** Hundreds since 2015
- **Pastors Killed:** Dozens documented

## Overview

Burundi is a small, densely populated East African nation that is approximately 90% Christian — one of the highest proportions in Africa. The Catholic Church has deep roots and significant institutional presence; Protestant denominations, including evangelicals and Pentecostals, have grown substantially. The vast majority of Burundians worship freely. However, Burundi is not without religious freedom concerns: Jehovah’s Witnesses have been banned since 2019, some evangelical groups face registration difficulties, and the political crisis that began in 2015 created an environment in which churches that spoke critically of the government faced pressure, surveillance, and intimidation.

## The 2015 Crisis and Church Suppression

When President Nkurunziza sought a disputed third term in 2015, mass protests erupted. Security forces violently suppressed the protests; an estimated 1,200 people were killed, 400,000 fled the country. Several Catholic bishops issued public statements opposing the third term. The government responded angrily, accusing the church of political interference. Some pastors and church leaders who sheltered protesters or spoke out publicly were threatened.

## Jehovah’s Witnesses and Minority Groups

In 2019, the government banned Jehovah’s Witnesses, ordering their dissolution and prohibiting their meetings. The stated reason was failure to comply with registration requirements, but independent observers agreed the government’s real concern was the group’s refusal to participate in political structures. Witnesses were arrested for holding meetings; their properties were seized. As of early 2025, the ban remained in effect. This represents a genuine and ongoing violation of religious freedom, regardless of the theological distinctions between the Witnesses and mainstream Christianity.

## Current Status

The overwhelming majority of Christians in Burundi — Catholic and mainstream Protestant — worship without significant interference. The concerns are real but targeted. Burundi is not in the Open Doors World Watch List’s top tiers.

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~90% (Catholic majority)
- **Primary Concerns:** Jehovah’s Witnesses ban (2019–present); political pressure on churches since 2015
- **Current Status:** Majority Christian community worships freely; specific groups face real restrictions
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not in top tier

The church has been rebuilt from almost nothing over the past three decades, now numbering 250,000–350,000 in a Buddhist-majority nation where legal freedom exists but social discrimination persists.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Cambodia, Southeast Asia, Buddhism, Khmer Rouge, Church Rebuilding, House Churches, Religious Freedom
PERSECUTION TIER: Moderate

## Overview

Cambodia’s Christianity carries one of the most dramatic survival stories in the modern church. The Khmer Rouge regime of 1975–1979 — one of the most ideologically extreme and brutally violent governments in human history — systematically dismantled every dimension of Cambodian civil and religious life. By 1979, organized Christianity in Cambodia had been essentially destroyed. What has happened since — the slow, painful rebuilding of a church from near zero — is a story of extraordinary resilience. Today, Cambodia’s Christian community numbers an estimated 250,000–350,000 in a nation of 17 million.

## Colonial-Era Christianity

Catholic missionaries arrived in Cambodia during the French colonial period. The Christian and Missionary Alliance established work in Cambodia beginning in 1923, making significant progress in translating scripture and training Cambodian church leaders. By the early 1970s, Cambodia had an estimated 60,000 Christians and a functioning institutional church.

## The Khmer Rouge Genocide (1975–1979)

The Khmer Rouge took power on April 17, 1975. Their revolution sought to construct “Year Zero” — a completely new society that rejected everything connected to the ancien régime. The genocide killed an estimated 1.7–2.2 million Cambodians — roughly a quarter of the population. Christianity was identified as a foreign religion connected to the French colonizers and Western imperialism. Churches were closed immediately. Buildings were demolished, repurposed, or burned. Clergy and church leaders were executed. By 1979, the church that had taken decades to build had been essentially eliminated.

## Rebuilding from Zero (1979–Present)

When the United Nations Transitional Authority in Cambodia (UNTAC) oversaw a democratic transition in 1991–1993, Cambodia opened to the outside world. Christian organizations entered the country, provided humanitarian assistance, and began supporting the rebuilding of the church. From near zero in 1979, Cambodia’s Christian population has grown to an estimated 250,000–350,000 today — representing perhaps 2–3% of the population.

## Legal Status and Social Challenges

Cambodia’s constitution guarantees religious freedom. Buddhism is recognized as the state religion and is deeply woven into Cambodian cultural identity, but the constitution does not prohibit other faiths. In some rural communities, where Buddhist identity and village social structure are tightly integrated, conversion to Christianity is viewed as a rejection of community and ancestors. Converts face family pressure, social ostracism, and occasionally more formal community-level sanctions. Pastors and church leaders occasionally report harassment from local officials.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** 250,000–350,000 (~2–3% of population)
- **Primary Faith Context:** Theravada Buddhist majority (~96%)
- **Primary Challenges:** Social pressure on converts, local-level harassment, Buddhist nationalist sentiment
- **Legal Status:** Formally free; constitution guarantees religious freedom
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not in top tier
- **Historical Trauma:** Khmer Rouge (1975–1979) systematically destroyed organized Cambodian Christianity; church rebuilt from near zero
