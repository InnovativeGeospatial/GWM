## Overview

Latvia is a small Baltic nation of approximately 1.8 million people with a historically divided Christian landscape: Lutheran in the north and center (a legacy of German Reformation influence through the Baltic German nobility), Catholic in the southeast (Latgale region, which fell under Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth influence), and a Russian Orthodox community among the ethnic Russian minority. All denominations practice freely. Latvia restored independence in 1991 after fifty years of Soviet occupation, which suppressed religious life severely — closing churches, imprisoning clergy, and promoting atheism through state institutions. The post-Soviet church has been rebuilding steadily in a society that remains moderately secular by Western European standards.

Latvia is not on the Open Doors World Watch List. The primary challenge facing Latvian Christianity today is not persecution but the cultural disengagement that follows decades of enforced atheism and the general secularizing drift of European society. A 2022 criminal case against a Lutheran pastor who preached on biblical sexual ethics drew some international attention from religious freedom advocates but was ultimately not prosecuted. Latvia is an EU member with strong constitutional religious freedom protections.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~55% (nominal; active practice lower)
- **Primary Denominations:** Lutheran (~35%), Catholic (~23%), Orthodox (~10%)
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Historical Note:** Soviet occupation severely suppressed religious life (1940–1941, 1944–1991); church rebuilding ongoing

## Overview

Lebanon is unlike any other Arab country in its relationship to Christianity. Christians — predominantly Maronite Catholics, Greek Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Armenian Catholic, Greek Catholic (Melkite), and a variety of Protestant denominations — constitute approximately 35–40% of Lebanon’s population, making it the most Christian Arab nation in the world. The Lebanese state itself was constructed around Christian political identity: the 1943 National Pact that established Lebanese independence allocated the presidency to a Maronite Christian, the prime ministership to a Sunni Muslim, and the parliament speaker to a Shia Muslim — a confessional political system premised on Lebanese Christianity’s political weight.

That architecture is under existential pressure. Christian emigration — driven by the 15-year civil war (1975–1990), repeated Israeli-Hezbollah conflicts, Syrian interference, and the catastrophic economic collapse that accelerated after 2019 — has been relentless. The October 2023 conflict between Hezbollah and Israel, which sent tens of thousands of Christians fleeing from southern Lebanon, accelerated an already severe demographic crisis. The Lebanese Christian community — ancient, diverse, and politically formative — faces an uncertain demographic and institutional future in a country that is itself disintegrating. Christianity in Lebanon is not persecuted by the state; it faces something in some ways more insidious: a slow, accumulated erosion through violence, poverty, displacement, and the failure of the very political system designed to protect it.

## The Ancient Maronite Church

The Maronite Church traces its origins to St. Maron, a 4th-century Syrian monk whose followers settled in the mountains of Lebanon and developed a distinctly Lebanese Christian identity rooted in the Lebanese mountain landscape, the Syriac liturgical tradition, and fierce independence from both the Byzantine and Arab worlds. When the Arab Islamic conquest swept through the Levant in the 7th century, the Maronites retreated to the mountains of northern Lebanon — the Qadisha Valley and Mount Lebanon — where their rugged terrain gave them protection that their lowland neighbors lacked. For over a thousand years, the Lebanese mountains were a Christian refuge in a Muslim-majority region.

The Crusader period brought the Maronites into closer contact with Rome; by the 12th century, the Maronite Church had entered into full communion with the Roman Catholic Church — a union that has been maintained ever since. This makes the Maronite Church the oldest Eastern Catholic church in continuous communion with Rome. The Maronite community sent scholars to Rome and produced some of the most important Arabic and Syriac Christian scholarship of the early modern period. The Maronite College in Rome, founded in 1584, trained generations of Maronite clergy who returned to Lebanon as educated leaders of their community.

The other major Christian communities — Greek Orthodox (the Byzantine tradition, historically the church of the eastern Mediterranean Roman Empire), Armenian (refugees from the 1915 genocide), and Greek Catholic (Melkite, in union with Rome since the 18th century) — add to Lebanon’s extraordinary Christian denominational diversity. This diversity within the Christian community has not always been a source of unity; the various communities have their own political affiliations, family networks, and historical grievances.

## The Lebanese Civil War and Christian Militias

Lebanon’s 15-year civil war (1975–1990) was one of the most complex and devastating conflicts in Middle Eastern history. Its origins lay in the fragile confessional political balance being overturned by Palestinian Liberation Organization (PLO) activity on Lebanese soil, the rapid growth of the Shia Muslim population (which had been underrepresented in the original confessional formula), and the intervention of Syria and Israel in Lebanese internal affairs.

For the Christian community, the war was both a period of intense self-defense and a period of moral catastrophe. The Phalange party — a Maronite Christian political-military organization — was one of the primary Christian armed actors. In September 1982, Phalangist militiamen — with Israeli forces controlling the surrounding area — entered the Sabra and Shatila Palestinian refugee camps in Beirut and massacred between 800 and 3,500 Palestinian civilians over three days. The massacre — condemned internationally as a war crime and found by an Israeli commission of inquiry to carry Israeli indirect responsibility — has defined the moral legacy of Christian Lebanese nationalism in a way the community has never fully escaped.

The war ended with the Taif Agreement in 1990, which redistributed political power — reducing the Maronite president’s powers and increasing Muslim representation — in ways that reflected the demographic shifts of the war period. It was a settlement the Christian community accepted under pressure, and one they have chafed under ever since.

## Hezbollah, Syrian Influence, and Christian Marginalization

The post-civil war period brought new threats to Lebanon’s Christians. Syria maintained military and political control over Lebanon until 2005, when the assassination of former Prime Minister Rafik Hariri triggered the Cedar Revolution and Syrian forces withdrew. The Syrian occupation had been particularly oppressive for Christian political figures who resisted it; General Michel Aoun spent fifteen years in exile after Syrian pressure forced him from Lebanon in 1990.

Hezbollah — the Iran-backed Shia Islamist organization and political party — has grown to become the most powerful military and political force in Lebanon, operating its own armed wing that is larger and better equipped than the Lebanese army itself. Hezbollah’s relationship with Lebanon’s Christians is complex: some Christian parties have allied with Hezbollah politically (Aoun’s Free Patriotic Movement formed a controversial alliance with Hezbollah in 2006); others, particularly the Lebanese Forces, regard Hezbollah as an existential threat to Lebanese Christian political identity and sovereignty.

For ordinary Lebanese Christians, Hezbollah’s dominance means living in a country whose most powerful actor is funded by Iran, whose military adventures (the 2006 war with Israel, which devastated southern Lebanon; ongoing support for Syria’s Assad) repeatedly expose Lebanon to devastation, and whose political stranglehold prevents the state reforms that might make Lebanon economically functional. The October 2023–2024 Hezbollah-Israel conflict caused new waves of displacement in the south and east — areas where Christians and Shia Muslims have lived in proximity for centuries.

## Economic Collapse and the Emigration Crisis

Lebanon’s economic collapse — which accelerated dramatically after 2019, with the lira losing over 90% of its value and the banking system effectively confiscating depositors’ savings — has driven unprecedented emigration across all Lebanese communities. For Christians, the emigration is particularly alarming because the Lebanese diaspora (concentrated in Brazil, the United States, Australia, France, and across West Africa) already contains far more Lebanese Christians than Lebanon itself does. The Lebanese diaspora is estimated at 8–14 million people; Lebanon’s population is approximately 5.5 million. The flow is relentlessly outward.

Educated Christians — doctors, engineers, professors, business professionals — leave at the highest rates because they have the most international options. The Lebanese Christian community that remains is aging, economically depleted, and increasingly desperate. Church institutions — schools, hospitals, charitable networks — that have served the community for generations are struggling to stay financially viable.

The August 2020 Beirut port explosion — caused by the detonation of approximately 2,750 tons of improperly stored ammonium nitrate — killed over 200 people, injured 6,000, and caused catastrophic damage to much of Beirut. The historic Christian neighborhoods of Gemmayzeh and Mar Mikhael were among the worst affected. Ancient churches and homes of historic significance were destroyed or severely damaged. The explosion — widely attributed to the criminal negligence of a political system dominated by sectarian interests — concentrated in its devastation everything wrong about Lebanon’s governance.

## Current Status

Lebanon’s Christian community today is stable in its institutions — the Maronite Patriarchate, the Greek Orthodox Patriarchate of Antioch (which has its actual seat in Damascus but its Lebanese patriarchal presence in Bkirki), and other Christian bodies maintain their organizational life — but demographically anxious and politically weakened. Christians are not persecuted by the Lebanese state, which still formally allocates the presidency to a Maronite. But the state itself barely functions; a presidential vacancy lasted over two years (2022–2025) as competing factions could not agree on a candidate. In that vacuum, Hezbollah governs the south, the economy continues to collapse, and Lebanese Christians debate whether their country’s future can include them.

The Lebanese Christian community’s contribution to Arab intellectual, cultural, and political life — through its universities, its press, its literature, its political philosophy — has been disproportionate to its size. The erosion of that community is a loss not only for Christians but for the entire Middle East.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~35–40% (Maronite, Greek Orthodox, Armenian, Melkite, Protestant)
- **Primary Concerns:** Hezbollah dominance undermining state sovereignty; economic collapse driving emigration; displacement from conflict; demographic decline
- **Current Status:** No state persecution; profound existential pressure from political collapse, economic catastrophe, and conflict
- **Open Doors WWL:** Monitored; mid-tier
- **Historical Significance:** Most Christian Arab nation; Maronite Church in continuous communion with Rome since 12th century; ancient Christian presence in Lebanese mountains since 7th century

## Overview

Lesotho is a small, landlocked kingdom entirely surrounded by South Africa, with a population of approximately 2.2 million — and approximately 90% of its population is Christian. The Catholic Church arrived through French missionaries (the Oblates of Mary Immaculate) in the 1860s at the invitation of King Moshoeshoe I, who sought European allies and was impressed by the missionaries’ practical assistance and education. The Paris Evangelical Missionary Society established the Lesotho Evangelical Church around the same time. Both institutions remain deeply embedded in Lesotho’s social fabric, running the majority of the country’s schools and hospitals.

Christianity is not only practiced freely in Lesotho — it is woven into the national identity. The country’s constitution declares it a Christian nation. There is no persecution, no notable restriction on any denomination, and no pattern of anti-Christian violence. Lesotho is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

## Overview

Liberia is approximately 85% Christian — with Baptist, Methodist, Pentecostal, and Catholic communities all significant — and has a unique founding story in the Christian world. Established in 1822 by the American Colonization Society as a settlement for freed American slaves, Liberia was built by people who carried their American Protestant Christian faith back to Africa. The republic these settlers founded in 1847 was explicitly Christian in its cultural orientation; the capital Monrovia was named after U.S. President James Monroe, and the founding population’s deep Baptist and Methodist faith shaped the country’s educational and civic institutions.

The relationship between the Americo-Liberian settler population (descendants of freed American slaves) and the indigenous African population was historically hierarchical and at times exploitative — a painful irony given the settlers’ own experience of slavery. This tension contributed to the conditions that produced Samuel Doe’s 1980 coup and the two devastating civil wars (1989–1997 under Charles Taylor’s insurgency and 1999–2003) that killed approximately 250,000 people and destroyed the country’s infrastructure.

Christianity in Liberia is deeply rooted in all communities — both the Americo-Liberian founding population and the indigenous groups, many of whom were evangelized through missions. The church was a significant institution of survival and reconstruction during the war years. Liberia is not on the Open Doors World Watch List; Christians practice freely. The country’s challenges are post-conflict recovery and governance rather than religious persecution.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~85%
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked

COUNTRY: Libya
TITLE: Libya — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/libya

CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Libya, North Africa, Militia Violence, ISIS, 21 Martyrs, Coptic Christians, Migrant Persecution, WWL Top 25, State Collapse
PERSECUTION TIER: High
WWL RANKING: #27 (Open Doors 2024)

## Overview

Libya’s Christianity story is shaped entirely by absence and catastrophe. The ancient Libyan church — which produced Tertullian and contributed to the Council of Nicaea — was extinguished by the Islamic conquest in the 7th century and never revived. The Libya of the modern era has no indigenous Christian population, no historic churches, and no institutional Christian presence of any kind. What it has, since the fall of Muammar Gaddafi in 2011, is a collapsed state, dozens of competing armed militias, a thriving human trafficking economy, and the most dangerous environment in the world for sub-Saharan African migrants — the majority of whom are Christian.

The February 2015 beheading of 21 Coptic Egyptian migrant workers by the Islamic State on a Libyan beach — filmed and released globally as ISIS propaganda, the victims’ final words “Lord Jesus Christ” audible as their executioners moved along the line — is the single most dramatic act of anti-Christian violence in the modern Middle East and North Africa. It made Libya’s horror visible to the world for a moment. The horror has continued, largely without that visibility, ever since.

## Ancient Christianity and Its Disappearance

The region of modern Libya — divided in ancient geography between Cyrenaica in the east and Tripolitania in the west — was thoroughly Christianized during the Roman period. Cyrene is mentioned in the New Testament: Simon of Cyrene carried Jesus’s cross to Golgotha (Mark 15:21). The church in Cyrenaica was significant enough to produce bishops who attended the great councils. Tertullian, who wrote in Carthage nearby, addressed letters to North African churches across the region. The Libyan church was real, established, and connected to the wider Mediterranean Christian world.

The Arab Islamic conquest of Libya was swift — completed by 647 AD — and the Christian community did not survive it as it survived in Egypt (where the Coptic Church endured) or as it endured in Lebanon (where the mountains provided protection). The reasons for this difference are partly geographic — Libya had no defensible mountain stronghold — and partly demographic. The indigenous North African Christian community was absorbed, converted, or dispersed. By the Fatimid period, organized Christianity in Libya had ceased to exist.

Italian colonization (1911–1943) brought a Catholic presence — an Italian settler community with churches, a cathedral in Tripoli, and Catholic institutions. When Italy lost Libya after World War II and the settler population departed, those institutions were left behind and fell into disuse. The modern Libyan state under both King Idris (1951–1969) and Gaddafi (1969–2011) had no significant Christian population to protect or to persecute.

## State Collapse and the Migrant Crisis

The NATO-backed uprising that killed Gaddafi in October 2011 removed the one institution — however brutal and corrupt — that had maintained central authority in Libya. What replaced it was a complex, shifting landscape of militias, regional power brokers, and competing governments (one backed by Turkey and Western powers; one backed by Russia, the UAE, and Egypt) that has never cohered into a functional state. In this vacuum, criminal networks — particularly human trafficking networks moving sub-Saharan African migrants northward toward the Mediterranean coast and the prospect of reaching Europe — established themselves with near-total impunity.

Sub-Saharan African migrants — predominantly from Nigeria, Chad, Niger, Eritrea, Ethiopia, Sudan, and West African countries — flow through Libya in hundreds of thousands. The majority are Christian. What they encounter in Libya is not a transit country but a predatory system: detention by militias, sale between criminal networks, forced labor, torture (including beatings, burns, and electric shocks documented by the United Nations), sexual violence, and in some cases execution. Those who cannot pay ransom demands are sold again or killed.

The specific targeting of Christians occurs in the context of militia groups with Islamist ideology, who identify Christian migrants as enemies of Islam and treat them with particular brutality. Christian migrants in militia detention have been compelled to renounce their faith, have had Bibles confiscated and burned, and have been subjected to Islamist indoctrination under duress. Others have been killed specifically because they would not deny Christ. The scale of this ongoing violence is enormous; its documentation is fragmentary because almost no independent journalists or human rights investigators can operate safely in the affected areas.

## The 21 Martyrs of Libya

On February 15, 2015, the Islamic State’s Libya affiliate released a video titled “A Message Signed With Blood to the Nation of the Cross.” It showed 21 Coptic Egyptian men in orange jumpsuits being marched along a beach near Sirte, Libya. They had come to Libya as migrant workers — construction workers, seeking employment to support their families in the villages of Upper Egypt. They had been kidnapped by ISIS months earlier.

In the video, each was forced to kneel on the beach as an ISIS fighter stood behind him with a blade. Some of the men’s lips moved in prayer as the executioners positioned themselves. A narrator announced their crime: they were “people of the cross, followers of the hostile Egyptian church.” As the blades fell, witnesses reported hearing the men cry out “Ya Rabbi Yasua” — “Lord Jesus Christ” — in their final breath.

All 21 were Coptic Christians. One — the one non-Egyptian — was Matthew Ayairga, a Ghanaian migrant worker who was not a Christian at the time of his capture but who reportedly declared himself a Christian after witnessing the faith of the Egyptian men with whom he was imprisoned. He was killed alongside them.

The Coptic Orthodox Church canonized all 21 as martyrs — the “Martyrs of Libya” — within weeks of their deaths. Their icons, showing orange-clad figures with palm branches and crowns, hang in Coptic churches worldwide. Their families in Beni Suef and Al Aour, Egypt, have spoken publicly about their faith and their sons’ deaths with a dignity and theological clarity that moved the global Christian community. The spot on the beach where they died was, after Libya’s political situation permitted, visited by relatives and later by Pope Francis’s envoy.

## Current Status

Libya ranked #27 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List. The ranking reflects not ongoing persecution of an indigenous Christian population — there is none — but the catastrophic conditions facing the hundreds of thousands of Christian migrants who transit or are trapped in Libya annually. There is no state protection, no recourse, and no accountability for those who kill, enslave, or torture them. The warring governments that nominally control parts of Libya have no capacity or will to address the situation. Christian migrants who enter Libya are, in large numbers, entering a country where their faith may cost them their lives.

The international response — concentrated on managing migration flows toward Europe rather than on the human rights of those trapped in Libya — has been widely criticized by human rights organizations and the Christian advocacy community.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #27
- **Indigenous Christian Population:** Effectively zero
- **Affected Population:** Sub-Saharan African Christian migrants in the hundreds of thousands annually
- **Primary Threats:** Militia violence, human trafficking, torture, forced recantation, killing of Christians in captivity
- **Legal Status:** No functioning state; no law enforcement for minority protection of any kind
- **Notable:** 21 Coptic Egyptian martyrs beheaded by ISIS, February 15, 2015 — canonized by the Coptic Church; one Ghanaian migrant embraced Christianity and died alongside them

## Overview

Lithuania is approximately 77% Roman Catholic — the most Catholic of the three Baltic states — and has full constitutional religious freedom. Christianity arrived in Lithuania in 1387 through the conversion of Grand Duke Jogaila, making Lithuania the last pagan nation in Europe to be Christianized. The Catholic faith became deeply embedded in Lithuanian national identity, and under Soviet occupation it played a role analogous to that of Catholicism in Poland: a vehicle of national resistance to communist rule.

Soviet repression of the Lithuanian Catholic Church was severe. Churches were closed, seminary enrollment was capped, priests were arrested and exiled, and all public religious activity outside approved worship was criminalized. The response was remarkable: beginning in 1972, a samizdat (underground) publication called The Chronicle of the Catholic Church in Lithuania began documenting religious persecution and distributing its findings clandestinely — inside Lithuania and across the Iron Curtain. Published continuously until 1989 — 81 issues — it was one of the most significant acts of religious civil disobedience of the Cold War era, produced by priests and laypeople who faced imprisonment for their participation. Several editors were arrested and sentenced to labor camps.

Since independence in 1990 and EU membership in 2004, Lithuania has full religious freedom. The Catholic Church has rebuilt its institutional presence. Lithuania is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~80% (predominantly Catholic)
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Historical Note:** Last pagan nation in Europe to convert (1387); Chronicle of the Catholic Church in Lithuania (1972–1989) — one of the most significant acts of religious civil disobedience under Soviet rule

## Overview

Madagascar is a large island nation in the Indian Ocean with a population of approximately 28 million, of whom roughly 65% identify as Christian (Catholic and Protestant in roughly equal measure) and approximately 35% as practitioners of traditional Malagasy religion or a combination of Christianity and indigenous spirituality. Christianity arrived primarily through the London Missionary Society in the early 19th century, initially under the patronage of the Merina monarchy before Queen Ranavalona I expelled missionaries and executed converts in the 1830s–1850s — a short-lived period of anti-Christian persecution that produced the first Malagasy Christian martyrs, canonized by the Catholic Church in 1989. After Ranavalona’s death in 1861, Christianity was fully restored and spread rapidly.

Today, Madagascar’s Christian community practices freely. The blending of Christianity with Malagasy ancestral religious practices (fomba gasy) is common and reflects the way the faith has been indigenized rather than indicating persecution. Madagascar is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

## Overview

Malawi is a small, landlocked East-Central African nation of approximately 20 million people, of whom approximately 82% identify as Christian. The Presbyterian Church of Central Africa (CCAP) — established through the Livingstonia and Blantyre missions founded by David Livingstone’s legacy — and the Catholic Church are the largest denominations, alongside growing evangelical and Pentecostal communities. Approximately 13% of Malawians are Muslim, concentrated in the south.

David Livingstone’s association with Malawi is particularly significant: he traveled through the country repeatedly between the 1850s and 1870s, was appalled by the slave trade he witnessed, and died in present-day Zambia in 1873 while still searching for the source of the Nile. His heart was buried in Africa; his body was carried by his companions — a journey of nine months — to the coast for transport to Westminster Abbey. Livingstone’s witness catalyzed the missionary movement that established the Christian institutions that built modern Malawi.

Malawi’s church is active, indigenous, and well-established. Christians practice freely. The country’s former long-serving president, Hastings Banda (1963–1994), required Jehovah’s Witnesses to carry party cards and subjected them to violent persecution — beatings, killings, and the burning of their literature. This persecution ended with Banda’s fall and the democratization of the 1990s. Today Malawi is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~82%
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked

## Overview

Malaysia is a constitutional monarchy with a Muslim-majority population — approximately 63% Muslim — in which Islam is the state religion under the federal constitution and in which a parallel dual-track legal system operates: civil law for all Malaysians and Sharia law for Muslim personal and family matters. Christians represent approximately 9–10% of the population — roughly 3 million people — concentrated primarily among the Chinese and Indian ethnic minorities on the peninsula, and among the indigenous Dayak, Kadazan-Dusun, and other peoples of Sabah and Sarawak in Malaysian Borneo, who converted to Christianity through 19th and 20th-century missions and are predominantly Christian.

Christians in Malaysia are legal and have churches, denominations, and active community life. They are not systematically imprisoned or physically attacked by the state. But they operate within a legal and social framework that systematically disadvantages them relative to the Muslim majority, uses legal mechanisms to restrict church activity, penalizes conversion from Islam with devastating social and legal consequences, and has tolerated and in some cases permitted organized Islamist pressure — including the burning of churches — without adequate accountability. Malaysia occupies a distinctive and important place in the global picture of Christian restriction: not the brutal persecution of Eritrea or Afghanistan, but the sophisticated use of law, regulation, and selective enforcement to constrain and diminish the church in a nominally democratic country.

## The Allah Controversy

The most internationally known legal battle involving Malaysian Christians is the “Allah controversy.” The word “Allah” — Arabic for God, used by Arab-speaking Christians across the Middle East for centuries and used by Malay-speaking Christians in Malaysia and Indonesia in their translations of the Bible — was banned from use by non-Muslims in Malaysia. The government’s position, upheld in a series of court decisions, was that the word “Allah” is exclusively Islamic and that its use by Christians could “confuse” Muslims and potentially lead to apostasy. The Malaysian Bible — the Al-Kitab — uses “Allah” throughout; the ban created a situation in which the government could seize and stamp Bibles imported for Christian use.

The Catholic Church’s newspaper The Herald challenged the ban; a 2009 High Court ruling found the ban unconstitutional; this was reversed by the Court of Appeal in 2013 and upheld by the Federal Court. The legal and social pressure around this issue has been an ongoing marker of the asymmetric treatment of Christians in the Malaysian legal system.

## Church Restrictions and Arson

Christian churches in Malaysia face significant construction barriers. Building a new church in a Muslim-majority area is extremely difficult; permits are routinely denied on technical grounds, and local opposition — organized by Islamist groups — can block construction for years or permanently. Existing churches that attempt to expand or relocate face similar obstacles.

In January 2010, following the initial court ruling in favor of the Herald newspaper’s use of “Allah,” a wave of attacks on churches occurred across Malaysia: eleven churches were attacked in four days, several of them firebombed. Islamist organizations publicly threatened violence before the attacks. The Malaysian government condemned the attacks, but the organizations that organized the threatening demonstrations faced no legal consequences. The message received by Malaysian Christians was that organized Islamist intimidation would be tolerated as long as it was not too lethal.

## Converts and the Sharia System

Malaysia’s dual legal system creates the most severe consequences for Muslims who convert to Christianity. Under the Sharia courts that govern Muslim personal law in each Malaysian state, apostasy is variously treated as requiring counseling, imprisonment, and/or formal repudiation that must be processed through the Sharia court — a process that in practice is rarely completed successfully. A Muslim who converts to Christianity and wishes to be legally recognized as a non-Muslim faces a legal labyrinth from which exit is effectively blocked in most states.

For ethnic Malays — who are constitutionally defined as Muslim (the Malay constitution defines “Malay” as someone who professes Islam, speaks Malay, and adheres to Malay customs) — conversion from Islam is not merely a religious act but an ethnic identity claim. A Malay who leaves Islam faces loss of the Bumiputera (“sons of the soil”) affirmative action benefits that accrue to ethnic Malays in education, employment, housing, and business. Conversion is thus economically, legally, and socially catastrophic for ethnic Malays in a way that has no parallel for Chinese or Indian Malaysians.

Pastors who disciple converts from Muslim backgrounds are at legal risk. Several have been arrested, had their churches raided, or been deported (for foreign nationals). The space for genuine Christian community with converts from Islam is severely constrained.

## Sabah and Sarawak: A Different Reality

The states of Sabah and Sarawak in Malaysian Borneo present a significantly different picture. These states — which joined the Malaysian federation in 1963 — have indigenous populations that are predominantly Christian and that have historically been more resistant to the Islamization pressures of peninsular Malaysia. The Dayak, Kadazan-Dusun, and Iban peoples of Borneo converted to Christianity through late colonial and post-colonial missionary work and maintain active, well-established churches.

The Malaysian federal government has at times pursued Islamization policies in Sabah and Sarawak that have alarmed the Christian majority there. The Allah ban — applied to the Al-Kitab used in Borneo’s churches — is acutely felt in communities where “Allah” is simply the word for God in their Christian heritage. Political tension between Sarawak and Sabah Christians and the federal government in Kuala Lumpur over religious rights has been a recurring feature of Malaysian politics.

## Current Status

Malaysia sits in the middle tier of the Open Doors World Watch List. Physical violence against Christians is sporadic rather than systematic; the more significant challenge is the accumulated weight of legal discrimination, construction restrictions, the Allah ban, the Sharia system’s treatment of converts, and the selective tolerance of Islamist pressure. The Malaysian church — particularly among Chinese and Indian Christians and among the indigenous peoples of Borneo — is active, growing, and determined. But it grows in a political environment that has been consistently tightening rather than loosening.

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~9–10% (~3 million)
- **Primary Threats:** Allah ban on non-Muslim use, church construction barriers, Sharia system crushing converts from Islam, police raids, deportation of foreign pastors, organized Islamist pressure on churches
- **Current Status:** Legal practice permitted; systematic legal and social discrimination; converts from Islam face severe consequences
- **Open Doors WWL:** Mid-tier ranked
- **Notable:** 2010 church arson wave (11 churches attacked); Al-Kitab Bible with “Allah” seized and stamped; Sabah and Sarawak indigenous Christians maintain stronger position than peninsular Christians

## Overview

The Maldives — a chain of coral island atolls in the Indian Ocean southwest of India, with a population of approximately 530,000 — is one of the most religiously uniform nations on earth. The constitution declares Islam the state religion, requires all citizens to be Muslim, and links citizenship itself to Islamic faith. There are no indigenous Maldivian Christians. There are no churches. There are no publicly visible non-Muslim Maldivians. A Maldivian who converts from Islam to Christianity does not merely face social stigma or legal penalties — they face the formal loss of their citizenship, rendering them stateless in their own country, cut off from family, property, employment, and all social standing. The Maldives is among the ten most restrictive nations on earth for Christianity, ranked #10 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List — not because of widespread violence against Christians but because of the near-total elimination of any legal space for the faith to exist.

## Islam in the Maldives

The Maldives converted to Islam in 1153 AD, when the ruling sultan accepted the faith following the work of an itinerant Muslim preacher. Prior to this, the Maldives was predominantly Buddhist. Since 1153, Islam has been the exclusive religion of the Maldivian people — an unbroken 870-year religious history. There was no pre-Islamic Maldivian Christianity to suppress; the faith never took root here. Portuguese colonization in the mid-16th century brought brief European contact (1558–1573), but the Maldivian resistance expelled the Portuguese without significant Christianization occurring.

The constitutional conflation of Maldivian identity with Islamic identity is thus not an arbitrary imposition — it reflects a genuine historical reality in which being Maldivian and being Muslim have been synonymous for nearly nine centuries. This does not make it a valid basis for religious freedom violations under international human rights standards, but it explains why the framework is deeply socially embedded rather than merely politically imposed.

## Expatriate Christians and the Tourist Economy

The Maldives is a significant tourist destination — its luxury resort islands are among the most visited in the world. A substantial expatriate workforce of Sri Lankans, Indians, Bangladeshis, and others — many of whom are Christian — works in the tourism and hospitality sector. These workers can practice their faith privately in their accommodations. They cannot build churches, conduct public worship, or display religious symbols. They certainly cannot engage in any form of outreach to Maldivian nationals. The resort islands are physically and legally separate from the inhabited islands; tourists interact with the Maldivian population primarily through this buffer of the resort economy.

## The Reality for Secret Believers

Open Doors and Christian advocacy organizations believe a small number of Maldivian nationals have converted to Christianity — the number is believed to be in the dozens to low hundreds. Their situation is one of extreme isolation. They have no community, no pastor, no church, no fellowship. They practice entirely alone, without any of the communal dimensions that are central to Christian faith. Contact with the outside Christian world occurs only through the internet, which is monitored. Discovery means loss of citizenship, family, and livelihood. The individual cost of conversion in the Maldives is among the highest on earth.

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #10
- **Indigenous Christian Population:** Effectively zero; possibly dozens of secret converts
- **Primary Legal Framework:** Constitution requires all citizens to be Muslim; conversion means loss of citizenship
- **Churches:** Zero
- **Expatriate Christians:** Permitted private worship only; no public practice or outreach
- **Historical Note:** Muslim since 1153 AD; no pre-existing Christian tradition suppressed — the faith has simply never been permitted

## Overview

Mali is a vast, landlocked West African nation straddling the Sahara and the Sahel, with a population of approximately 23 million. Approximately 90% of Malians are Muslim; Christians represent roughly 5%, concentrated almost entirely in the south — in and around Bamako (the capital) and in the agricultural regions near the borders with Guinea, Ivory Coast, and Burkina Faso. For decades, Mali’s predominantly southern Christian community existed in a state of relative tolerance within the broader Muslim-majority society, rooted in a West African tradition of interreligious coexistence and undergirded by the secular state founded at independence in 1960.

That coexistence has been shattered. Since 2012, when Tuareg separatists and then jihadist groups seized northern Mali and the French military intervention (Operation Serval/Barkhane) began, Mali has been in a state of cascading collapse. The jihadist insurgency — dominated by Jama’at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin (JNIM), al-Qaeda’s regional affiliate, and supplementary ISIS-aligned groups — has spread from the north through the center and is pushing into the south. JNIM has conducted systematic campaigns against Christian communities — burning churches, beheading pastors, massacring Christian villages, and demanding that communities choose between conversion and death. The French military withdrew in 2022 following political pressure from the military junta that had seized power, replaced by Russian Wagner forces (Africa Corps) that have conducted their own documented atrocities. Mali ranked #20 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List and is trending worse.

## Christianity’s Historical Presence in Mali

The lands of modern Mali were among the great civilizations of medieval West Africa — the empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai — all of which became predominantly Muslim through the influence of trans-Saharan trade networks that brought Islamic scholars, merchants, and eventually rulers from North Africa. The city of Timbuktu was one of the great centers of Islamic scholarship in the medieval world; its libraries housed hundreds of thousands of manuscripts in Arabic and other languages. Christianity had no ancient presence in this region.

Modern Malian Christianity arrived through French colonial missions in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Catholic and Protestant missionaries — particularly the White Fathers (Missionaries of Africa) — worked primarily among the southern agricultural communities who were not yet deeply Islamized. Converts came primarily from among the Dogon, Bambara, Bobo, and other southern groups whose traditional religions had not been fully replaced by Islam. The Christian community that developed was small, rooted in the south, and institutionally tied to mission schools and hospitals. At independence in 1960, Mali was declared a secular state; Christians were recognized as a legitimate minority and practiced freely.

## The Jihadist Insurgency and Its Assault on Christians

The 2012 Tuareg rebellion in northern Mali, quickly hijacked by Islamist groups including Ansar Dine and al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM), established the template for what would follow. The jihadists imposed strict sharia in northern cities — destroying Sufi shrines in Timbuktu (which they regarded as idolatrous), banning music, and establishing Islamic courts. When French forces intervened in January 2013 and drove the jihadists from the major northern cities, the groups dispersed into the countryside and reconstituted as JNIM — a more disciplined, politically sophisticated coalition with a broader regional strategy.

JNIM’s strategy has included explicit and deliberate targeting of Christian communities in central and southern Mali. The pattern is the same as in Burkina Faso: militants arrive in a village, identify Christian residents or leaders, and conduct executions that serve both to eliminate Christian presence and to terrorize remaining communities into compliance. Pastors are among the first targeted — they are the visible leaders of Christian community life, and killing them decapitates the community. Churches are burned — not as incidental collateral damage but as deliberate religious erasure.

Documented attacks include: the killing of multiple pastors in the Mopti and Ségou regions in 2019–2020; the burning of dozens of churches across central Mali; the massacre of Christian villagers in attacks that followed the established pattern of separating Christians from Muslims before executing the former; and the forced displacement of thousands of Christians from areas that fall under JNIM territorial influence. By 2022, an estimated tens of thousands of Malian Christians had been displaced from their home communities in the center and center-south of the country.

## The Government Response and the French Withdrawal

Mali’s government — which has undergone two military coups (2020 and 2021), leaving Colonel Assimi Goïta in power as the head of a transitional junta — has been unable to protect civilian communities from jihadist violence. The French military’s decade-long presence (2013–2022) kept the jihadist movement from seizing major cities but did not reverse its expansion across the countryside. The junta’s decision to expel French forces and turn to the Wagner Group for military support has not improved the security situation for civilians; Wagner operations have been associated with documented atrocities against rural communities.

The Malian state’s capacity to project authority beyond the capital and the largest cities is extremely limited. Christians in rural areas of the center and south are essentially unprotected. They cannot call police who will not come; they cannot turn to a military that is neither present nor capable; they cannot rely on a government that is itself struggling for survival.

## The Church Today

Mali’s Christian community — small to begin with — is being compressed into urban areas as rural Christians flee jihadist-controlled or jihadist-threatened zones. In Bamako, churches remain open and active; international Christian organizations maintain offices; the Catholic Church and various Protestant denominations maintain institutional life. But the church’s reach into the rural areas where jihadist expansion is occurring is shrinking.

Christian leaders in Mali describe a situation of acute fear: pastors who will not abandon their flocks but who know that visibility makes them targets; congregants who continue to gather for worship knowing that discovery of a Christian community can bring a JNIM attack; families who have lost fathers, brothers, and sons to jihadist killings and continue to follow Jesus anyway. The Malian church is small, poor, under-resourced, and under assault — and it is not collapsing.

- **Current Status:** Urban Christians maintain institutional life; rural Christians facing severe violence and displacement
- **Government Protection:** Effectively absent in jihadist-active areas; military junta governs by coup
- **Notable:** Dozens of churches burned since 2019; multiple pastors beheaded; tens of thousands displaced

## Overview

Mauritania is a vast, sparsely populated Saharan and Sahelian nation of approximately 4.7 million people — and it is, by virtually every measure, one of the most closed nations on earth to Christian faith. The constitution declares Mauritania an Islamic republic; Islam is the state religion; all citizens are Muslim by law; apostasy is punishable by death under the Mauritanian Penal Code; proselytism is a criminal offense; and Christian literature — including Bibles — is illegal to import or possess in quantity. There are no public churches, no registered Christian organizations, and no indigenous Mauritanian Christian community of any visible scale. There may be a small number of secret converts whose existence is known only through the reports of advocacy organizations that cannot verify numbers. The Moors, Wolof, Soninke, Pulaar, and other ethnic communities of Mauritania have been Muslim for centuries — Islam arrived in the region in the 8th century, and Mauritania’s Muslim identity is not a superficial imposition but a deeply embedded social reality.

## Historical Background

Mauritania lies in the westernmost stretch of the Sahara and was part of the great empires of medieval West Africa — Ghana, Mali, Songhay — all of which were shaped by the trans-Saharan Islamic trade networks that brought Islam westward from North Africa. The Almoravid movement — one of Islamic history’s most significant reform movements — originated in what is now Mauritania in the 11th century. Islam has been the faith of the peoples of this region for over a millennium; Christianity had no historical foothold here in any period.

French colonial rule (1904–1960) brought French Catholic administrators and a small colonial religious presence, but the colonial church served French personnel rather than the Mauritanian population. Independence in 1960 established an Islamic republic from the outset; the constitution has never recognized religious pluralism for citizens.

## The Death Penalty for Apostasy

Mauritania’s penal code, revised in 2017, explicitly prescribes the death penalty for apostasy — making Mauritania one of a small number of countries where this sentence exists in codified law. Prior to the 2017 revision, apostasy was punishable under customary Islamic jurisprudence applied by courts, but the legislative codification represented a hardening rather than a relaxation of the framework. In practice, documented executions of apostates in Mauritania are rare — international pressure typically results in imprisonment rather than execution being carried out — but the law stands as the ultimate expression of the state’s position on conversion from Islam.

The 2014 case of blogger Mohamed Cheikh Ould Mkhaitir — sentenced to death for apostasy after writing critically about the use of religion to justify caste discrimination — drew significant international attention. He was held for years; the sentence was eventually reduced to a prison term through a legal process that satisfied neither religious conservatives who wanted execution nor international human rights advocates who wanted acquittal. He was released in 2019. The case illustrated both the existence of the death penalty and the political pressure that prevents its consistent application.

## Expatriate Christians and Migrants

A small expatriate community — primarily West African migrants, development workers, and diplomatic personnel — includes Christians who can worship privately in their homes or in designated spaces within diplomatic compounds. They cannot build churches, hold public services, distribute Christian materials, or engage Mauritanian nationals in any form of religious discussion. The Catholic Church maintains a small presence for expatriates; it operates with extreme discretion.

West African migrant workers from Christian-background communities (Nigeria, Senegal’s Christian minority, Guinea’s Christian communities) pass through Mauritania as a transit country on migration routes northward. Their faith is private and invisible in a country where open non-Muslim religious identity is illegal.

## Current Status

Mauritania ranked #24 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List. The ranking reflects the near-total absence of any legal space for Christianity alongside the death penalty apostasy law that makes the consequences of conversion from Islam potentially fatal. As with the Comoros and the Maldives, the ranking in the Open Doors top tier reflects comprehensive exclusion rather than widespread visible violence — primarily because there are so few Christians to persecute.

- **Indigenous Christian Population:** Effectively zero; possibly tiny number of secret converts
- **Legal Framework:** Apostasy death penalty codified in 2017 penal code; proselytism criminal; Christian literature illegal
- **Churches:** Zero public churches
- **Expatriate Christians:** Private worship only in diplomatic compound settings
- **Notable:** Mohamed Cheikh Ould Mkhaitir — sentenced to death for apostasy (2014); released 2019 after international pressure

## Overview

Mexico is approximately 83% Christian — roughly 68% Catholic and 15% evangelical Protestant — and one of the most Christian countries in North America by numbers. The state guarantees religious freedom, and no national-level government persecution exists. Yet Mexico appears on the Open Doors World Watch List because Christians face two distinct and documented threats: targeted cartel and criminal organization violence against pastors who challenge criminal authority in their communities; and the persecution of indigenous evangelical converts by community authorities who enforce traditional religious customs — typically a syncretic blend of Catholic and pre-Columbian spiritual practice — and expel converts from their villages, confiscate their property, and sometimes physically attack them.

## Christianity in Mexico

Spanish colonization brought Catholicism to Mexico with enormous force from 1519 onward. The evangelization of indigenous populations by Franciscan, Dominican, and Jesuit missionaries was both genuine and coercive — millions were baptized, indigenous religions were suppressed, temples were destroyed. The indigenous appropriation of Catholic faith — evident in the significance of the Virgin of Guadalupe, whose apparition to the indigenous peasant Juan Diego in 1531 created one of the most important Marian traditions in the world — reflects the complex synthesis that developed. By independence in 1821, Mexico was thoroughly Catholic in cultural identity.

The 19th and 20th centuries brought conflict between the church and the secular liberal state — most dramatically in the Cristero War (1926–1929), in which Mexican Catholics took up arms against the anti-clerical provisions of the revolutionary constitution. The Cristero War produced numerous martyrs, including Blessed Miguel Pro S.J., a Jesuit priest executed by firing squad in 1927. Pope John Paul II beatified him and 24 other Cristero martyrs in 1988; 13 more were canonized in 2000.

Evangelical and Pentecostal Christianity has grown dramatically since the mid-20th century, particularly in southern states including Chiapas and Oaxaca, and among urban poor communities across the country.

## Indigenous Community Persecution

The most distinctive form of Christian persecution in Mexico is the expulsion of evangelical converts from indigenous communities in Chiapas, Oaxaca, and other southern states. These communities — whose religious practice blends Catholic tradition with pre-Columbian spiritual customs, enforced by village authorities (the usos y costumbres system) — regard conversion to evangelical Protestantism as a rejection of community identity and solidarity. Community feasts, festivals, and public rituals are central to social and agricultural life; they are funded through community contributions and involve alcohol use that evangelical converts decline on religious grounds. When evangelical converts refuse to contribute financially or participate in these rituals, community authorities — elected under the traditional governance system — respond with sanctions escalating from fines to expulsion.

Expelled evangelical families lose their homes, their land, their access to community water and electricity, and their children’s enrollment in village schools. In some documented cases, they are physically attacked; in others, their belongings are seized or destroyed. The government of Mexico has struggled to address this pattern because the indigenous governance system and usos y costumbres are protected under Mexican law as expressions of indigenous autonomy. Federal courts have at times ordered the restoration of expelled converts; local implementation of such orders is inconsistent.

The state of Chiapas, where this pattern is most documented, has seen thousands of evangelical converts expelled from their communities across decades. The expelled communities form new settlements outside their original villages — a painful testimony to the cost of conversion.

## Cartel Violence Against Pastors

Mexico’s drug trafficking organizations — the Sinaloa Cartel, CJNG (Jalisco New Generation Cartel), and others — control large portions of Mexican territory and treat any organized community leadership that challenges their authority as a threat. Pastors who preach against drug trafficking, who run rehabilitation programs for addicts or former cartel members, who provide community moral authority in cartel-controlled areas, or who refuse cartel demands for cooperation face the same threats as their counterparts in Colombia, Honduras, and Guatemala.

Mexico’s pastor-killing rate is documented by Open Doors as among the highest in Latin America. The violence is concentrated in states with high cartel activity: Guerrero, Michoacán, Zacatecas, Chihuahua, and the Pacific coast region. Pastors are threatened, extorted, displaced, and killed. The perpetrators are criminal organizations, not the state — but the state’s inability or unwillingness to protect community leaders in cartel-controlled areas is itself a governance failure that enables the violence.

## Current Status

Mexico occupies the lower tier of the Open Doors World Watch List — not a country of systematic state persecution, but one where two distinct and serious patterns of anti-Christian violence affect thousands of Christians annually. The evangelical community is growing despite the pressure — precisely the communities most targeted by indigenous expulsions and cartel violence are often those with the most vibrant and determined evangelical church life.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~83%
- **Primary Threats:** Cartel violence against pastors in criminal-controlled territory; indigenous community expulsion of evangelical converts in Chiapas and Oaxaca
- **Current Status:** Full national religious freedom; regional and community-level persecution serious and documented
- **Open Doors WWL:** Lower tier ranked
- **Historical Note:** Cristero War (1926–1929) produced canonized martyrs; Virgin of Guadalupe (1531 apparition) is one of the most significant Marian traditions in Christianity

## Overview

Mongolia is one of the most remarkable stories of modern Christian growth in the world. In 1990, when the communist Mongolian People’s Republic dissolved, Mongolia had an estimated zero to a few dozen indigenous Christians — the result of seven decades of communist rule that had suppressed Buddhism, shamanism, and any other religious expression. By 2023, Mongolia is estimated to have approximately 60,000 Christians — almost all first- or second-generation converts, the children of a church that did not exist a generation ago. The church grew from nothing through the work of foreign missionaries who entered Mongolia in the early 1990s and found a population spiritually depleted by communism and open to something new.

Today, Mongolia’s Christian community navigates a changing environment: a government that has grown increasingly concerned about “foreign religious influence” and has tightened registration requirements; a Buddhist and shamanist majority culture that regards Christianity as a foreign intrusion into Mongolian identity; and the challenge of building a self-sustaining indigenous church out of a community with no pre-existing Christian tradition. The Mongolia story is not yet one of severe persecution — it sits in the moderate tier — but the trajectory of government religious control is toward restriction rather than openness, and the church faces real and growing pressure.

## The Post-Communist Opening

Mongolia’s peaceful democratic revolution in 1990 — one of Asia’s most successful political transitions from communist to democratic governance — opened the country to the outside world. Among those who came were Christian missionaries: South Koreans in the largest numbers, but also Americans, Europeans, and others. They found in Mongolia what missionaries rarely find: a population that had lost its traditional religious framework through communist suppression (the monastic Buddhist institutions that had dominated Mongolian life before 1921 were destroyed; an estimated 30,000 Buddhist monks were killed in communist purges of the 1930s) and that was neither hostile to new ideas nor committed to defending an existing religion.

The missionary strategy was multifaceted: English-language teaching programs, business development, medical missions, and direct church planting. Mongolian Christians who came to faith began evangelizing their own families and communities — the most effective means of church growth in any culture. By the late 1990s, there were hundreds of Mongolian Christian congregations, and the church was growing at rates that attracted global Christian attention as one of the most significant mission stories of the era.

## The Challenge of Identity and Indigenization

Christianity’s growth in Mongolia has faced the question of identity from the beginning: is Christianity a Mongolian faith or a foreign import? The Buddhist and shamanist traditions, though suppressed under communism, have been recovering since 1990 and have re-established themselves as the natural spiritual home of Mongolian identity. Buddhist leaders and some nationalist politicians have framed Christianity as a cultural invasion — a vehicle for Korean or Western values that threatens Mongolian distinctiveness.

This framing is not without political traction. Mongolia has passed and revised religious law multiple times since 1990, and the general direction of each revision has been toward greater state control. A 2020 Law on Combating Foreign Influence gave the government expanded powers to monitor and restrict foreign-funded religious activity. Christian organizations that receive funding from overseas — as most do — are formally required to declare that funding and are subject to scrutiny that domestically funded Buddhist institutions do not face.

## Registration Requirements and Restrictions

All religious organizations in Mongolia must register with the government. Unregistered religious activity is illegal. The registration process is bureaucratically complex and has been used selectively to create obstacles for smaller or newer Christian congregations. Some congregations have been denied registration or had it revoked. Foreign missionaries require special visas that are increasingly difficult to obtain and maintain; several have been deported in recent years.

The practical effect is a Christian community that is institutionally more fragile than its numbers might suggest. There are approximately 60,000 Christians in Mongolia — not a trivial number in a country of 3.3 million — but they lack the established institutions, trained leadership, and financial self-sufficiency of churches in countries with longer Christian histories. The dependence on foreign missionaries and foreign funding creates the vulnerability that nationalist politicians and government regulators exploit.

## The Indigenous Mongolian Church

The most significant development in Mongolian Christianity is the emergence of an indigenous church — Mongolian pastors, Mongolian theologians, Mongolian worship music, Mongolian evangelists. The translation of the Bible into modern Mongolian was completed in 1990 — almost exactly when the country opened — and has been revised and widely distributed. Mongolian Christian worship has developed its own musical idiom; Mongolian Christian literature is being written; Mongolian churches are sending their own missionaries to other parts of Asia and to Mongolian diaspora communities in China and Russia.

This indigenization is the church’s most important long-term asset. The government can restrict foreign missionaries; it cannot restrict Mongolian citizens practicing their faith. The more thoroughly Mongolian Christianity becomes — in its leadership, its funding, its cultural expression — the more resistant it becomes to the “foreign religion” critique that is the primary rhetorical tool of its opponents.

## Current Status

Mongolia is not in the Open Doors top tier. The concerns are real but moderate: registration requirements, restrictions on foreign missionaries, Buddhist nationalist pressure, and government surveillance of foreign-funded religious organizations. Physical violence against Christians is rare. The primary pressure is bureaucratic and social — not lethal. But the trajectory matters: each revision of the religious law has tightened control rather than loosened it, and the growth of Buddhist and shamanic nationalism creates a social environment less welcoming to Christianity than the open post-communist moment of the 1990s.

The Mongolian church — young, indigenous, growing, and increasingly led by Mongolians who have never needed a foreign missionary to tell them what they believe — is one of the most hopeful stories in global Christianity. Whether the political environment will allow it to grow to maturity or will succeed in strangling it with bureaucratic restriction is the open question that makes Mongolia worth watching.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~60,000 (~1.8%)
- **Primary Threats:** Registration requirements, restrictions on foreign missionaries, Buddhist nationalist pressure, government monitoring of foreign-funded religious organizations
- **Current Status:** No acute persecution; growing regulatory pressure; church is young and building institutional independence
- **Open Doors WWL:** Monitored; moderate tier
- **Growth Story:** From effectively zero Christians in 1990 to 60,000 by 2023 — one of the fastest church growth rates in the world over that period
- **Key Asset:** Bible translated into modern Mongolian in 1990; indigenous leadership developing rapidly

## Overview

Morocco is a constitutional monarchy in which Islam is the state religion, the king bears the title “Commander of the Faithful,” and religious identity is so thoroughly intertwined with national law and social expectation that to be Moroccan is, by legal and social default, to be Muslim. Christianity has no legal indigenous presence in Morocco. The small number of Moroccan nationals who have come to faith in Christ — believed to number between 2,000 and 8,000, though verification is impossible — practice in absolute secrecy, with no churches, no community structures they can maintain openly, no pastoral training they can pursue legally, and no legal protection from the consequences of their faith being discovered. Morocco ranked #28 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List.

## Ancient Christianity, Arab Conquest, and the Empty Saharan Church

The region of modern Morocco was part of the Roman province of Mauretania Tingitana, and Christianity was present from the early centuries of the church. The Bishop of Caesarea Mauretaniae (in modern Algeria, near the Moroccan border) was among the bishops who signed the Nicene Creed in 325 AD. There were Christian communities in the cities of Volubilis, Tingis (modern Tangier), and Sala (modern Salé). The martyrs of Tangier — killed under Diocletian in the 3rd century — are among the early North African church’s honored dead.

As in Algeria and Tunisia, the Arab Islamic conquest of the 7th century and the subsequent Berber adoption of Islam eliminated the Christian community of Morocco over the following centuries. By the Almoravid period in the 11th century, organized Christianity in Morocco had ceased to exist. Unlike Egypt, where the Coptic Church maintained continuity through seventeen centuries of Islamic rule, the North African church west of Egypt simply disappeared — leaving behind no surviving indigenous Christian tradition, only ruins and the names of bishops in ancient conciliar records.

Portuguese and Spanish colonization in the 15th and 16th centuries established small coastal Christian presences, and French colonial rule (1912–1956) brought the Catholic Church primarily as a settler institution. The post-independence framework under King Mohammed V established Morocco as an Islamic state; the Catholic Church was permitted to maintain its presence for the expatriate population but was explicitly prohibited from any outreach to Moroccan nationals.

## The Legal Framework Against Conversion

Article 220 of the Moroccan Penal Code criminalizes “impeding the exercise of religion or of religious ceremonies, or disturbing the practice of a religious group when done in a way that troubles public order” — language that has been applied to evangelism. More practically relevant is the criminalization of “shaking the faith of a Muslim” — proselytism directed at Muslims is a criminal offense with penalties of imprisonment and fines. Foreigners who engage in outreach to Moroccan nationals are arrested, detained, and deported; this has occurred repeatedly with foreign NGO workers and missionaries discovered with materials intended for Moroccan nationals.

For Moroccan converts, apostasy is not explicitly criminalized in the penal code but is treated as a grave offense under customary Islamic jurisprudence applied in family law matters. A Moroccan who converts to Christianity cannot legally change their religion on their national identity documents; they continue to be classified as Muslim for all legal purposes including marriage, divorce, inheritance, and child custody. Their children are legally Muslim. If discovered by family members or authorities, they face family violence, police interrogation, loss of employment, and potential prosecution on other pretexts. Several documented cases involve Moroccan converts being arrested, interrogated, and pressured to renounce their faith before being released.

## The Underground Church

The Moroccan underground church is small, scattered, and operating under extreme pressure, but it is real and — by the testimony of those who have left the country and speak from a position of safety — growing. Communities of Moroccan believers meet in small groups, rarely more than five or ten people, in private homes. Leadership is informal. Bibles and Christian literature circulate through trusted networks. No records are kept that could identify members. Prayer and worship happen quietly, without music that might be audible through apartment walls.

The growth of the underground church in Morocco is connected to several developments: satellite television broadcasting of Christian content in Arabic and Moroccan Arabic (Darija); the internet and social media, which connect isolated converts with a broader community and with Christian resources; and the testimony of Moroccan workers and students who encountered Christianity while in Europe and returned with their faith. Morocco’s Amazigh (Berber) communities — who have a distinct ethnic identity from Arab Moroccans and a history of viewing Arab-Islamic identity with some ambivalence — have been among those most receptive to Christianity in some reports.

## Expatriate Christians

The Catholic and Protestant expatriate communities in Morocco — primarily French, Spanish, sub-Saharan African students, and diplomats — can worship in designated churches in Casablanca, Rabat, and other cities. These churches are visible, legal, and active; they serve the foreign community and have done so since the colonial period. They cannot accept Moroccan national members, cannot conduct services in Darija for a Moroccan audience, and cannot engage in any form of outreach to Moroccan nationals. Sub-Saharan African students — many of whom are Christian — can attend these churches as non-nationals.

The expulsion of sub-Saharan African migrants from Morocco in 2013–2014 affected large numbers of Christian Africans who had been attending churches in northern Morocco. The mass expulsions were primarily an immigration enforcement action but disrupted established Christian communities.

## Current Status

Morocco ranked #28 on the 2024 World Watch List. The situation for indigenous Moroccan Christians is one of total legal exclusion and significant personal risk. The situation has not improved under King Mohammed VI’s reign, which has maintained the colonial-era arrangement of permitting expatriate Christian practice while prohibiting any outreach to nationals. The underground church grows slowly, quietly, and at significant cost to those who comprise it.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #28
- **Estimated Indigenous Christian Population:** 2,000–8,000 (all secret believers; unverifiable)
- **Primary Threats:** Criminalization of proselytism, apostasy consequences (family rejection, legal jeopardy, loss of civil status), deportation of foreign missionaries, no legal recognition of Moroccan Christian identity
- **Legal Status:** Article 220 Penal Code criminalizes evangelism; apostasy not explicitly criminal but devastating in practice
- **Churches:** None for Moroccan nationals; expatriate churches exist for foreign nationals only
- **Notable:** North Africa was once the intellectual heartland of Christianity (Tertullian, Cyprian, Augustine’s tradition); today has essentially zero indigenous Christian presence

## Overview

Mozambique presents a familiar Sahelian pattern transplanted to Southeast Africa. The country is approximately 57% Christian — the majority belonging to Zionist and Apostolic African Independent Churches, Catholic, and Protestant denominations — with approximately 18% Muslim and the remainder following traditional religions. In the south and center of the country, Christians practice freely. But in Cabo Delgado — the remote, gas-rich northern province bordering Tanzania — an Islamic State-affiliated insurgency that local people call Al-Shabaab (unrelated to the Somali group, but inspired by similar ideology) has since 2017 conducted a sustained campaign of violence that has killed thousands, displaced over a million people, and specifically targeted Christian communities and churches.

## Christianity in Mozambique

Portuguese colonization established Catholicism in Mozambique from the 16th century, primarily in coastal trading settlements. After independence in 1975, the Frelimo (Marxist liberation movement) government was hostile to religion and nationalized church institutions, expelled missionaries, and restricted religious activity. The situation moderated over time; by the 1990s, Mozambique had shifted toward formal religious freedom. The decades since have seen significant church growth across all denominations. African Independent Churches — Zionist, Apostolic, and charismatic movements with distinctly African theological and cultural expression — are particularly prominent.

## The Cabo Delgado Crisis

Cabo Delgado is Mozambique’s northernmost province, bordering Tanzania on the Indian Ocean coast. It is one of the country’s poorest regions despite the discovery of vast offshore natural gas deposits that have attracted massive international investment. An insurgency began in 2017 when a group known as Ansar al-Sunna Wa-Jamma — locally called Al-Shabaab — attacked a police station. The group has since been formally affiliated with the Islamic State (as ISCAP, Islamic State Central Africa Province) and has conducted a sustained campaign of atrocity: beheadings, village burnings, kidnapping, and the creation of a humanitarian crisis of staggering scale.

For Christian communities in Cabo Delgado, the violence has been explicitly religiously targeted. Churches have been burned — dozens in the affected region. Pastors have been killed. Christians have been beheaded in public executions. In some cases, militants have entered villages, separated Christians from Muslims, and executed the Christians. The pattern of execution is consistent with the methodology of jihadist groups across the Sahel and in the DRC.

The fall of the port town of Mocímboa da Praia to the insurgents in 2020 was a major escalation. The insurgents held it for over a year, establishing a proto-state with its own sharia governance, courts, and social control. Rwandan military forces and Mozambican army operations drove the insurgents from Mocímboa da Praia in 2021, but the insurgency dispersed into the interior and has continued its attacks, including operations that have extended southward toward Pemba (the provincial capital) and into Niassa Province.

An estimated 1 million people have been displaced from Cabo Delgado — one of Africa’s significant humanitarian crises, largely invisible in international media. Among the displaced are many of the province’s Christian communities, whose churches and villages have been burned.

## Current Status

Mozambique sits in the lower tier of the Open Doors World Watch List — not a top-tier persecution country overall, but with a specific, serious, and growing crisis in Cabo Delgado that demands monitoring. The overwhelming majority of Mozambican Christians worship freely and are not under threat. The Cabo Delgado crisis is geographically concentrated but catastrophic within its geography, and the pattern of jihadist southward expansion — seen in Mali, Burkina Faso, and now Mozambique — gives grounds for serious concern about what the next decade brings for the church in this country.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~57%
- **Primary Threat:** ISIS-affiliated ISCAP insurgency in Cabo Delgado — church burnings, pastoral killings, village massacres, Christian displacement
- **Displacement:** ~1 million from Cabo Delgado region
- **Current Status:** South and center fully free; Cabo Delgado in acute crisis
- **Open Doors WWL:** Lower tier ranked (Cabo Delgado crisis)
- **Notable:** ISCAP explicitly separates Christians from Muslims for execution; dozens of churches burned since 2017

The 2021 coup transformed the country into a war zone — and the Christian communities of the Chin, Kachin, Karen, and Kayah states are paying in blood.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Myanmar, Burma, Southeast Asia, Military Junta, Tatmadaw, Chin Christians, Kachin, Karen, Kayah, WWL Top 15, Church Bombing, Ethnic Persecution

## Overview

Myanmar is a nation of profound ethnic diversity whose majority Bamar Buddhist population has never fully accepted the existence of ethnic minority peoples who maintain distinct identities, languages, cultures — and, for several of the largest minorities, Christian faith. The Chin, Kachin, Karen (Kayin), Kayah (Karenni), and Zomi peoples are predominantly Christian, converted through 19th and 20th century Baptist and Catholic missionary work, and their Christianity has become as central to their ethnic identity as Buddhism is to Bamar identity. Since the military coup of February 1, 2021 — which removed the civilian government of Aung San Suu Kyi and returned the Tatmadaw (military) to absolute power — Myanmar has been in a state of multi-front civil war, and the military has conducted systematic atrocities against civilian populations in these Christian ethnic minority regions: burning churches, bombing villages, killing pastors, and using sexual violence against women and girls as a deliberate weapon of war. Myanmar ranked #13 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List, and the situation has deteriorated significantly since the 2021 coup.

## The Ancient Majority and Its Religious Identity

The Bamar — the dominant ethnic group, approximately 65–70% of the population — are Theravada Buddhist in a way that integrates spirituality, ethnicity, and national identity as thoroughly as any society in the world. Burmese Buddhism is not merely a religion; it is the container of Bamar cultural memory, political authority, and social order. The monastic institution, the sangha, commands extraordinary reverence. The concept of merit (kutho), earned through generosity to the monks and religious observance, shapes daily life. The relationship between Buddhism and Bamar political authority has deep historical roots going back to the Pagan Empire of the 11th–13th centuries.

The ethnic minority peoples of the country’s periphery — the hills and jungle regions of the Chin, Kachin, Karen, Kayah, and Shan states — were never fully incorporated into this Bamar Buddhist world. They maintained their own languages, customs, and — through the colonial period — their own religious traditions, which were disrupted by missionary Christianity in ways that proved transformative.

## American Baptist Missions and the Christian Minorities

Adoniram Judson, an American Baptist missionary, arrived in Burma in 1813 and worked primarily among the Burman population with limited success. His breakthrough came among the Karen people — who had a prophetic tradition that some interpreted as anticipating the arrival of a teacher bearing a sacred text. Karen converts became fervent evangelists among their own people; through the 19th and early 20th centuries, the Karen, Kachin, Chin, Kayah, and Zomi peoples converted to Christianity in large numbers through a combination of missionary education, indigenous evangelism, and the social advantages that literacy and mission school education provided.

By Myanmar’s independence in 1948, the Christian minorities had a distinctive identity: literate (the missionaries had created written scripts for previously unwritten languages and translated the Bible), educated (mission schools had given them advantages in the colonial administrative system), and organized around churches that served as community institutions. Christianity was not merely a religion for these communities — it was a marker of identity, a network, and an institutional foundation for community life.

## Independence, Military Rule, and Ethnic Conflict

Burma’s independence was followed by almost immediate ethnic conflict. The Karen demanded their own state; other minorities were similarly restive. The Tatmadaw — the military — became the institution most committed to Bamar Buddhist national unity and most hostile to ethnic minority self-determination. Successive military governments pursued a policy of Burmanization: imposing the Burmese language, suppressing minority languages, and treating Buddhism as the natural religion of the nation.

This produced decades of low-intensity warfare in the ethnic minority areas that has never fully stopped. The Kachin Independence Army (KIA), the Karen National Union (KNU), the Chin National Front (CNF), and various other armed ethnic organizations have been in conflict with the Tatmadaw for decades. Peace agreements have been partial, temporary, or unimplemented. Through all of these conflicts, the Christian character of these communities — their churches, their pastors, their networks — has been both a target of Tatmadaw hostility and a source of communal resilience.

## The 2021 Coup and Escalation

The February 2021 coup brought the Tatmadaw back to absolute power and triggered a national uprising. The Civil Disobedience Movement (CDM), the People’s Defence Force (PDF) guerrillas, and the existing ethnic minority armed organizations combined to create a multi-front resistance that the military has fought with brutal counterinsurgency tactics. In the Christian ethnic minority areas, this has meant:

**Church bombings:** The Tatmadaw has bombed churches in Chin, Kachin, Karen, and Kayah states with airstrikes. The Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception in Mindat, Chin State, was burned. Dozens of churches across the Christian minority regions have been bombed, burned, or shelled. In some cases, civilians who had taken refuge inside churches were killed in airstrikes. This is not incidental — churches are community gathering points and are identified by the military as potential resistance meeting points.

**Village burnings:** Military forces have systematically burned villages in Chin, Kayah, and Karen states. Entire Christian communities have been driven from their homes. The village burnings are documented by satellite imagery, survivor testimony, and international human rights organizations. Some villages have been burned multiple times as displaced residents tried to return.

**Killing of pastors and religious leaders:** Multiple pastors have been killed by the Tatmadaw — arrested, tortured, and executed, or killed in airstrikes and ground assaults. Christian community leaders are specifically identified as threats because they are trusted authorities in communities the military is trying to control or suppress.

**Sexual violence:** The Tatmadaw’s use of rape and sexual violence as a weapon of war against ethnic minority women — documented extensively over decades — has continued and intensified since 2021. Reports from Chin, Kayah, and Karen states describe gang rape by soldiers, forcing families to witness assaults, and sexual violence used to terrorize communities into displacement. This is systematic, not incidental.

**Displacement:** Over 2 million people have been internally displaced in Myanmar since the 2021 coup (as of early 2025). A significant proportion are from Christian ethnic minority communities in the affected states. They have fled to forests, to India (which borders Chin State), to Thailand, and to internally displaced person (IDP) camps where humanitarian access is limited.

## The Church Under Fire

The Myanmar church — particularly the Baptist conventions that serve the Chin, Kachin, Karen, and other communities — is one of the most deeply tried Christian communities in the world today. Pastors who have stayed with their congregations rather than flee face the real possibility of arrest, torture, and death. Some have been killed. Others have led their communities through the jungle to find safety, functioning as pastors, community organizers, and trauma counselors simultaneously.

The Kachin Baptist Convention, the Karen Baptist Convention, and the Chin Christian organizations maintain contact with international partners and advocate for their communities’ survival. Their stories — rarely covered in international media — document a church that is suffering severely, that has not been broken, and that continues to baptize new believers in rivers in the jungle while on the run from military operations.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #13
- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~6–9% of 54 million (~3–5 million, concentrated in ethnic minority regions)
- **Primary Threats:** Military (Tatmadaw) airstrikes on churches, village burnings, killing of pastors, sexual violence as a weapon of war, mass displacement
- **Most Affected Communities:** Chin (predominantly Christian), Kachin (~85% Christian), Karen and Kayah (significant Christian populations)
- **Displacement:** 2+ million since 2021 coup; significant proportion from Christian minority communities
- **Historical Foundation:** American Baptist missionary Adoniram Judson (arrived 1813); Karenic, Chin, Kachin peoples converted in 19th–20th centuries through indigenous evangelism following missionary education
