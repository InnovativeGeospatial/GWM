## Overview

Cameroon is often called “Africa in miniature” for the diversity compressed within its borders — savanna and rainforest, Francophone and Anglophone regions, Muslim north and Christian south. Approximately 70% of Cameroonians identify as Christian, 20% as Muslim, and 10% as adherents of traditional religions. The Christian majority worships largely freely, and the country has historically been one of West-Central Africa’s more stable and pluralistic environments. But two converging crises have made Cameroon increasingly dangerous for Christians in specific regions: the Boko Haram insurgency in the Far North region, which has attacked churches and Christian villages near the Lake Chad basin; and the Anglophone crisis in the Northwest and Southwest regions, where armed separatist conflict has created a humanitarian disaster that has ravaged Christian communities.

## Historical Roots of Christianity

Christianity arrived in Cameroon through multiple channels. Catholic missionaries — primarily German and Swiss — came in the late 19th century under the German colonial administration, and the Basel Mission established Protestant work in the coastal region. British Baptist missionaries had already been active on the coast from the 1840s, with Alfred Saker founding the first Baptist mission in Douala in 1845. When Germany lost its African colonies after World War I, Cameroon was divided between British and French administration — a division that produced the French-speaking majority and English-speaking minority whose tensions would resurface catastrophically in the 21st century.

Christianity spread deeply through both colonial halves of the country. Catholic institutions — schools, hospitals, and churches — became foundational to social infrastructure in the south. Presbyterian, Baptist, and other Protestant missions created a strong tradition of educated, organized Anglophone Protestant communities in the west. By independence (1960 for French Cameroon, 1961 for British Southern Cameroons), Christianity was the dominant faith in the south and center, while Islam remained dominant in the north.

## Islam in the North and Boko Haram

Cameroon’s Far North region borders Nigeria and Lake Chad and has historically been Muslim under the influence of the Fulani-led Sokoto Caliphate and successor states. Christians in this region — numbering perhaps 10–15% of the local population — have always lived as a minority, and while relations with Muslim neighbors were not always without tension, outright persecution was not the norm.

Boko Haram’s expansion from Nigeria changed this dramatically. From approximately 2014 onward, Boko Haram and its splinter faction ISWAP (Islamic State West Africa Province) conducted raids into Cameroon’s Far North, targeting military posts, civilian villages, and churches. Kidnappings for ransom became common — including the abduction of Christian aid workers, pastors, and civilians. Churches in border communities have been burned. Christians have been killed in village raids in which attackers distinguished between Muslim and Christian residents. The Cameroonian military has fought Boko Haram in the Far North with some effectiveness, but the border is long, porous, and difficult to secure, and attacks have continued.

## The Anglophone Crisis

The Anglophone crisis is one of Africa’s least-reported humanitarian emergencies. It began in 2016 when Anglophone teachers and lawyers protested the imposition of French-language courts and curricula in the English-speaking Northwest and Southwest regions. The government’s heavy-handed crackdown on protesters radicalized a segment of the population, and armed separatist groups coalesced around the goal of creating an independent state called Ambazonia. The Cameroonian military launched a counterinsurgency campaign that has been documented — by Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, and others — to include extrajudicial killings, burning of villages, and torture of detainees.

The crisis is not religious in its origins — it is linguistic, constitutional, and ethnic. But its victims are overwhelmingly Christian, because the Anglophone regions are predominantly Christian (Presbyterian, Baptist, Catholic). Churches have been caught between armed separatist groups, who demand boycotts of schools and businesses on pain of violence, and the military, which has at times fired on civilian targets. Pastors have been abducted by separatists demanding money; others have been killed. Church buildings have been burned — sometimes by the military, sometimes by separatist factions. Schools founded by churches have been shuttered for years, either because separatists banned school attendance or because teachers fled. An estimated 600,000 people are internally displaced in the Anglophone regions, with hundreds of thousands more having fled to Nigeria.

The Presbyterian Church of Cameroon, one of the country’s oldest and most respected institutions, has had pastors killed, church buildings burned, and congregations displaced. Catholic parishes have been similarly affected. The crisis has no resolution in sight as of early 2025.

## Current Status

Cameroon is not in the Open Doors World Watch List top tier but sits on the boundary. The majority Christian population in the south and center worships freely. The pressures are regional and specific — Boko Haram violence in the Far North, and the Anglophone crisis in the northwest and southwest — but in those regions, the effects on Christian communities are severe. The church in Cameroon is bearing significant suffering, largely out of view of the international community.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~70%
- **Primary Threats:** Boko Haram violence (Far North); Anglophone crisis displacing and killing Christians in Northwest/Southwest
- **Current Status:** Majority worships freely; specific regions experiencing serious violence
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not in top tier; Anglophone crisis and Boko Haram activity are monitored concerns
- **Displaced:** ~600,000+ in Anglophone regions alone

## Overview

Canada is a majority-Christian country — approximately 63% of Canadians identified as Christian in the 2021 census, though active church attendance is significantly lower — with full constitutional protection of religious freedom under the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. Christians in Canada are not persecuted by any international standard. The country ranks among the world’s strongest guarantors of religious liberty and has no history of state-directed violence or systematic oppression of Christians.

Canada is not on the Open Doors World Watch List, nor should it be. However, the country’s recent Christian history includes several notable developments that GWM users may encounter: the residential schools reckoning and the wave of church arsons that followed, pandemic-related court battles over church closures, and ongoing legal disputes over the limits of religious expression in public life.

## Christianity in Canada

Christianity came to Canada through French Catholic and British Protestant colonization. The Catholic Church dominated French Canada — Quebec in particular — from the colonial era through the early 20th century, shaping education, family life, culture, and politics in ways that have few parallels in the English-speaking world. The Quiet Revolution of the 1960s produced one of the most dramatic and rapid secularizations in modern history: Quebec went from being among the most observant Catholic societies in the West to among the least in under two generations.

English Canada developed a Protestant-dominated civic culture through the 19th and early 20th centuries, shaped by Anglican, Methodist, Presbyterian, and later Baptist and Pentecostal communities. Mass immigration in the 20th century — particularly from Southern and Eastern Europe, Asia, and the Caribbean — diversified the religious landscape substantially. Today, Canada is genuinely pluralistic, with Islam, Sikhism, Hinduism, and Buddhism all representing significant and growing communities.

## The Residential Schools Crisis and Church Arsons

In May 2021, ground-penetrating radar surveys of former residential school sites in British Columbia identified what were initially reported as the unmarked graves of 215 children at the former Kamloops Indian Residential School. The story generated national and international attention and reopened profound wounds about the residential school system — a network of church-run (primarily Catholic, Anglican, and United Church), government-funded schools that separated Indigenous children from their families from the 1880s through the 1990s, with the explicit goal of “Christianizing and civilizing” them. The system caused documented and devastating harm — cultural destruction, physical abuse, sexual abuse, and death from disease and neglect.

In the months following the Kamloops announcement, more than 80 Catholic and Protestant churches across Canada — many on or near Indigenous reserves — were burned or vandalized. The arsons were widely understood as acts of anger directed at institutions associated with the residential school system. No deaths resulted, but the destruction of historic church buildings was significant, and Indigenous communities that used those buildings for their own worship were among those harmed.

The residential schools legacy is a genuine moral reckoning. The churches involved — particularly the Catholic Church — have issued apologies and engaged in processes of accountability. Pope Francis visited Canada in July 2022 and issued a formal apology on Canadian soil. The complexity of this history — Christianity as both a tool of colonial harm and a source of spiritual life for many Indigenous Canadians who practice it today — is important context for understanding Canadian Christianity.

## Legal and Cultural Tensions

Canada’s legal landscape involves ongoing tension between religious freedom claims and anti-discrimination frameworks. Courts have adjudicated cases involving Christian adoption agencies that declined to place children with same-sex couples, Trinity Western University’s attempt to open a law school with a community covenant on sexual conduct, and street preachers whose methods of expression were deemed threatening. These are genuine legal debates in a society navigating competing rights claims — they do not constitute persecution.

Pandemic church closures (2020–2022) generated significant conflict, including extended legal battles in Alberta led by Pastor James Coates of GraceLife Church and the famous “Aylmer pastor” cases. Some of these situations involved heavy-handed enforcement. Courts generally upheld public health restrictions as constitutional, with some exceptions.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~63% (declining; active attendance significantly lower)
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no systematic persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Notable Issues:** 80+ church arsons (2021) following residential schools news; ongoing legal debates over religious expression; pandemic-era church closure conflicts

## Overview

The Central African Republic — one of the world’s poorest and most fragile states — has experienced a decade of catastrophic sectarian violence that has left the church scarred and the country in ruins. The crisis began in 2013 when a predominantly Muslim coalition of armed groups called Séléka seized the capital Bangui and overthrew the government. What followed was a spiral of atrocity: Séléka fighters targeted Christian communities across the country, killing civilians, burning churches, and driving hundreds of thousands from their homes. A counter-force of predominantly Christian and animist militias, the Anti-Balaka, formed in response and committed their own atrocities against Muslim communities. The violence fractured the country along religious lines in ways it has never fully recovered from. A decade later, armed groups still control large swaths of the CAR, and Christian communities in the interior continue to face violence, displacement, and destruction.

## Christianity in the CAR

Christianity arrived in the Central African Republic through French colonial missions in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Catholic and Protestant — particularly Baptist — missions established churches, schools, and medical work that became foundational to social life. By independence in 1960, Christianity was the dominant faith in the south and center of the country, and the church had genuine institutional depth through its educational and medical networks.

The CAR was always a fragile state — poor in infrastructure, dependent on subsistence agriculture, and governed through cycles of coup and instability since independence. But the religious demography was not historically a source of acute conflict. The Muslim population — concentrated in the north and northeast, linked to trans-Saharan trade routes and Sahelian Islamic traditions — coexisted with the Christian and animist south. The country’s various ethnic groups maintained their own dynamics, and while local tensions existed, the kind of organized religious violence that emerged in 2013 was not a natural outgrowth of prior history. It was introduced and weaponized.

## The Séléka Crisis (2013–Ongoing)

Séléka — a name meaning “alliance” in Sango, the national language — was a coalition of predominantly Muslim armed groups that included fighters from Chad and Sudan as well as Central African rebels. They swept into Bangui in March 2013, forcing President François Bozizé into exile. The forces that constituted Séléka were poorly disciplined, ideologically heterogeneous, and driven by a combination of political grievance, ethnic interest, and — for some factions — jihadist ideology.

In the areas they controlled, Séléka fighters conducted a campaign of systematic violence against Christian populations. Churches were burned — in some areas systematically, not opportunistically. Priests and pastors were killed. Christian villages were looted and their inhabitants killed or driven out. Catholic and Protestant mission compounds — which served as community anchors for healthcare and education — were attacked and their staff threatened or killed. The scale was staggering: by 2014, an estimated one million people — roughly a quarter of the country’s population — had been displaced.

The Anti-Balaka militias that arose in opposition to Séléka committed their own atrocities, primarily against Muslim civilians and communities. They drove most of the Muslim population out of Bangui and from significant areas of the south and west. The conflict became self-reinforcing: each atrocity generated counter-violence, and the category of “Christian” and “Muslim” became lines of survival or death regardless of what any individual actually believed.

## The Humanitarian Scale

The human cost of the CAR crisis remains staggering. The United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) has consistently listed the CAR among the world’s top humanitarian emergencies. Key figures include: approximately 3 million people — out of a population of around 5 million — in need of humanitarian assistance; over 700,000 internally displaced; over 600,000 CAR refugees in neighboring countries (Chad, Cameroon, DRC, Congo Republic).

For the Christian community specifically: hundreds of churches have been destroyed, damaged, or seized. Catholic missions that had functioned for over a century were looted and burned. The Archbishop of Bangui, Cardinal Dieudonné Nzapalainga — who became internationally recognized for his courage in mediating between communities and sheltering civilians during the worst violence — has been both a witness to and a symbol of the church’s suffering and resilience.

## Russian Wagner Influence and Continued Instability

Since approximately 2018, the Russian Wagner Group (now rebranded as the Africa Corps) has been the CAR government’s primary military partner, displacing French forces and United Nations peacekeepers as the effective guarantors of the government’s survival. Wagner forces have been documented by UN investigators as committing atrocities against civilians — including Christian communities — in their operations against armed groups. The relationship between Wagner and specific armed groups has shifted over time, creating a complex and lethal landscape in which the civilian population — Christian and Muslim — has borne the cost.

A coalition of armed groups called the CPC (Coalition of Patriots for Change) launched an offensive in late 2020 that was repelled with Wagner assistance, but the groups remain active in portions of the north, east, and center. Christians in areas these groups control or contest face violence, extortion, and displacement.

## The Church Today

Despite a decade of devastation, the church in the CAR has not collapsed. In Bangui, Catholic and Protestant congregations meet and worship. The church has been among the most active voices for peace and reconciliation — Cardinal Nzapalainga, along with Muslim and Protestant leaders, formed a platform for interreligious dialogue that has been internationally recognized. Church-run schools and health facilities, though many were destroyed, have in some cases been rebuilt.

In the interior, the situation is harder. Pastors in armed-group-controlled areas practice at risk. Congregations have been scattered by displacement. Churches have been burned. Open Doors, Caritas, and other Christian humanitarian organizations continue to provide assistance and document ongoing violations.

The CAR’s ranking at #32 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List reflects not only active anti-Christian violence but the structural collapse of state protection, the continued presence of armed groups targeting Christians, and the profound humanitarian need of a Christian community that has been traumatized for over a decade.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #32
- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~89% (Catholic ~34%, Protestant ~55% — though significant Muslim minority)
- **Primary Threats:** Armed groups (Séléka factions, CPC coalition), ongoing sectarian violence, state collapse
- **Displacement:** 700,000+ internally displaced; 600,000+ refugees in neighboring countries
- **Churches:** Hundreds destroyed, damaged, or seized since 2013
- **Notable:** Cardinal Nzapalainga — Archbishop of Bangui — is an internationally recognized voice for peace and interreligious dialogue

## Overview

Chad is divided roughly in half between a predominantly Muslim north and a predominantly Christian and animist south — a geographic and cultural split that has shaped the country’s politics, conflicts, and religious life since independence in 1960. Christians represent approximately 44% of the population; Muslims approximately 52%. The two communities have coexisted with periodic tension, but Chad has not descended into the kind of systematic sectarian violence seen in the Central African Republic or Sudan. The primary threats facing Christians are Boko Haram violence in the Lake Chad basin, Islamic law applied by custom in northern communities, social pressure on converts from Islam, and the profound fragility of a state that has been governed under military-linked regimes for most of its history.

## Christianity in Chad

Catholic and Protestant missionaries established the foundation of Chadian Christianity during the French colonial period — the White Fathers in the south, and Protestant missions (primarily Brethren) in the center. Christianity took strong root among the Sara and other southern ethnic groups, who constitute the demographic base of the country’s Christian community. The south became the center of Chadian educated and administrative class under colonial rule, creating a historical link between Christianity, literacy, and state service that generated lasting tension with the Muslim north.

Since independence, Chad has been governed almost continuously by northern Muslim leaders, creating a political dynamic in which the Christian south has often felt politically marginalized despite its size and institutional development.

## Boko Haram and Lake Chad Basin Violence

Chad’s Lake Chad basin — the region bordering Nigeria, Niger, and Cameroon — has been a theater of Boko Haram and ISWAP violence since approximately 2015. Chadian military forces have been among the more effective in the Multinational Joint Task Force countering Boko Haram, and Chad has suffered significant military casualties. Civilian communities in the lake basin — including Christian fishing communities on the islands and shores of Lake Chad — have been attacked, displaced, and killed. Churches in this region have been burned. Christians have been specifically targeted in some raids.

## Converts and Social Pressure

Christians who come from Muslim backgrounds face severe social consequences. Chad has no formal apostasy law in its penal code, but Islamic customary practice in northern communities treats apostasy as a serious offense. Converts risk family rejection, loss of inheritance, loss of housing, and in some cases physical violence. Pastors who disciple converts from Muslim backgrounds are targeted with threats and sometimes violence. This pressure is not state-enforced at the national level but is socially enforced at the community level with significant effectiveness.

## Current Status

Chad is not in the Open Doors top tier but is monitored. The southern Christian community practices largely freely in its own territory. The threats are concentrated in the Lake Chad basin (Boko Haram violence), the north (Islamic customary pressure on converts), and in the social consequences for individuals who leave Islam. The overall direction — given the fragility of the state, the growth of Sahelian jihadism, and the political dominance of northern Muslim elites — is not toward greater security for Christians.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~44%
- **Primary Threats:** Boko Haram violence (Lake Chad basin); social persecution of converts from Islam; political marginalization of the Christian south
- **Current Status:** Southern Christians largely free; northern Christians and converts face serious pressure
- **Open Doors WWL:** Monitored but not top tier

## Overview

Chile is overwhelmingly Christian — approximately 65% Catholic and 17% evangelical Protestant, with a growing segment identifying as non-religious. The country has full constitutional religious freedom; no denomination faces persecution, and the state is formally secular. The most significant challenge facing the Chilean church in recent decades has not been external persecution but internal reckoning: the Catholic Church’s clergy sexual abuse scandal reached catastrophic proportions in Chile, leading to mass resignations, papal intervention, and a crisis of institutional trust that dramatically accelerated secularization.

Christianity came to Chile through Spanish colonization in the 16th century. The Franciscan, Dominican, and Jesuit orders established missions throughout the country, and Catholicism became the cultural and legal foundation of colonial Chilean society. Protestant Christianity came primarily through 19th-century German immigration and British influence, and Pentecostalism arrived in the early 20th century and has grown dramatically. Chile is home to some of the largest Pentecostal congregations in Latin America.

Chile is not on the Open Doors World Watch List and is not a country of concern for Christian persecution by any monitoring standard.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~82%
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Notable:** Catholic clergy abuse scandal produced significant institutional crisis and accelerated secularization

CATEGORY: Country Profiles

## Overview

China presents one of the most complex and consequential Christian situations in the world. The country is home to an estimated 97 million Christians — a number that, if accurate, makes China’s Christian population larger than the population of most European nations. It is also a country with one of the most comprehensive state systems for controlling, monitoring, and suppressing unsanctioned religious expression in the world. The Chinese Communist Party (CCP) does not oppose Christianity as such — it tolerates and manages approved, state-controlled Christianity through the Three-Self Patriotic Movement (TSPM) and the Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association (CPCA). What it cannot tolerate is Christianity — or any institution — that operates outside its control, grows beyond its visibility, or expresses loyalty to any authority above the Party. That is the fundamental tension driving Christian persecution in China: not the faith itself, but the question of who controls it.

## The Historical Arc of Christianity in China

Christianity has reached China multiple times across history. Nestorian Christianity arrived via the Silk Road in the 7th century AD — the famous Nestorian Stele, erected in Xian in 781 AD and rediscovered by missionaries in the 17th century, records the presence of a functioning Nestorian Christian community during the Tang Dynasty. Mongol rule in the 13th century brought another period of Nestorian and Catholic presence — figures at Kublai Khan’s court were Christian, and Franciscan missionaries reached China in this era.

Modern Christianity’s roots in China trace primarily to the Jesuit missions of the 16th and 17th centuries. Matteo Ricci’s approach of cultural accommodation — learning Chinese, adopting Confucian intellectual frameworks, engaging the emperor’s court — produced a sophisticated and initially successful mission. The Rites Controversy, in which Rome condemned the Jesuit practice of allowing Chinese Christians to continue ancestral rites, damaged the mission and ultimately led to the expulsion of missionaries in the early 18th century.

Protestant missions arrived in earnest in the 19th century. Robert Morrison of the London Missionary Society arrived in 1807 and produced the first Chinese Protestant Bible translation. Hudson Taylor founded the China Inland Mission in 1865 with the explicit goal of reaching China’s interior — he and his missionaries adopted Chinese dress, language, and customs in a way that was transformative for missionary practice globally. By the early 20th century, significant missionary networks — Catholic, Protestant, and evangelical — had established churches, schools, hospitals, and universities across China. Christianity was a significant cultural force, and several leaders of the Republican era had Christian connections, most famously Chiang Kai-shek.

## The Communist Revolution and the Three-Self Church

The CCP’s victory in 1949 ended the era of foreign missionary presence. Missionaries were expelled; Chinese Christians who had studied or worked with foreign missions were pressured to sever ties. The Three-Self Patriotic Movement was established in 1954 as the official Protestant body — “three-self” referring to self-governance, self-support, and self-propagation, principles originally articulated by 19th-century Protestant mission theory but repurposed by the CCP to mean state-controlled and independent of foreign influence.

The Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) was catastrophic for all religion. Churches were closed, ransacked, or destroyed. Bibles were burned. Clergy were persecuted, imprisoned, and in some cases killed. Public religious practice of any kind was banned. Christians worshiped entirely in secret, if at all. The period produced enormous suffering — and, paradoxically, a deepening of faith among those who maintained it under pressure.

After Mao’s death and the opening of China under Deng Xiaoping, religion was officially tolerated again within the state-approved framework. The Three-Self church reopened. The underground Catholic Church — which had maintained loyalty to Rome rather than joining the CPCA — continued in tension with both the official church and the state. And the house church movement — which had survived the Cultural Revolution through secret gatherings — began to grow rapidly and visibly.

## The House Church Movement

China’s house churches are the heart of what is likely the world’s fastest-growing major Christian community. These are unregistered congregations that operate outside the Three-Self Patriotic Movement framework, ranging from small family prayer groups to sophisticated urban networks with thousands of members, trained pastoral leadership, and theological depth. The most famous was Shouwang Church in Beijing, which in the 2010s conducted public outdoor worship rather than comply with registration requirements — a act of defiance that resulted in numerous arrests and years of legal pressure.

House churches exist across a spectrum. Some are theologically conservative and ethnically Chinese, tracing their lineage through the underground networks of the Cultural Revolution. Others are charismatic and evangelical, shaped by Korean and Western influence. A remarkable movement among Chinese diaspora has also produced theologically trained pastors who return to serve underground congregations.

The CCP’s fundamental objection to house churches is not theological but institutional: they represent organized civil society outside Party control, with loyalty structures (to scripture, to God, to a global church) that compete with loyalty to the Party and state. The larger they grow, the more acutely the Party feels this challenge.

## Xi Jinping’s “Sinicization” Campaign

Under President Xi Jinping, who came to power in 2012, religious policy has significantly tightened. The concept of “Sinicization of religion” — articulated clearly from 2015 onward — requires that all religious practice in China be adapted to “Chinese culture” and “socialist core values” as defined by the Party. In practice, this has meant:

Regulations on Religious Affairs revised in 2018 tightened registration requirements, banned religious education of minors, restricted online religious content, required that religious personnel demonstrate political loyalty, and expanded state oversight of religious organizations’ finances and operations.

A cross-removal campaign that began in Zhejiang Province in 2014 and spread nationally removed the visible crosses from thousands of church buildings — including Three-Self churches — on the grounds that they were “too prominent” or violated building codes. The campaign affected an estimated 1,500–2,000 churches in Zhejiang alone.

Increased surveillance of church activities, including facial recognition at church entrances, CCTV monitoring, and requirements that churches submit member lists to the authorities. Children under 18 have been formally banned from Sunday school and religious instruction in many regions.

Closure and demolition of churches: in some areas, entire church buildings have been demolished. The demolition of the Sanjiang Church in Zhejiang Province in 2014 — a large, officially approved Three-Self building — demonstrated that even registered churches were not immune to state action when they were deemed too prominent.

Arrest and imprisonment of house church leaders: Pastor Wang Yi of Early Rain Covenant Church in Chengdu was arrested in December 2018, along with approximately 100 members of his congregation, in a coordinated raid. He was sentenced to nine years in prison on charges of “inciting subversion of state power” and “illegal business operation.” He remained imprisoned as of early 2025. Numerous other pastors have been imprisoned on similar charges.

## The Catholic Situation

The Vatican’s 2018 provisional agreement with China — which gave Beijing a formal role in the appointment of Catholic bishops — was deeply controversial within the Catholic community. The underground Catholic Church, which had maintained loyalty to Rome through decades of persecution, felt abandoned. The agreement was intended to unify the official and underground Catholic communities under a framework acceptable to both Rome and Beijing, but implementation has been contested, and the underground church — now technically supposed to integrate with the official CPCA — faces ongoing uncertainty.

## Bibles and Scriptures

Bibles are not legally printed in China for general distribution — they can only be printed by state-approved publishers (primarily the Amity Foundation, a TSPM-linked body) in limited quantities and distributed through registered churches. Importing Bibles is technically controlled; large-scale imports are intercepted and confiscated. In practice, Bibles circulate widely through informal channels, but the legal restriction means that access to scripture is a tool of state control over religious life.

In 2019, reports emerged that the government had removed Bible sale listings from major Chinese e-commerce platforms including Taobao and JD.com. This was not a complete Bible ban but a further tightening of distribution channels.

## The Church That Cannot Be Stopped

Despite the comprehensive nature of CCP control, the Chinese church continues to grow. Estimates of China’s Christian population range from 38 million (official TSPM figures) to 97 million (Pew Research and other independent estimates) — the wide range reflects both genuine uncertainty and the definitional question of what counts as a committed Christian versus a nominal one. Even the lower estimate represents extraordinary growth from near-zero after the Cultural Revolution.

The resilience of the Chinese church is a documented historical reality. The Cultural Revolution attempted total suppression; the church emerged from it larger and more committed than it entered. The Sinicization campaign has imprisoned thousands, demolished churches, and removed crosses — and the church continues to baptize new believers. The house church movement is decentralized in ways that make it structurally difficult to fully suppress. Its leadership is trained, its theology is serious, and its members understand that their faith has a cost.

China ranked #15 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List. For a country of 1.4 billion people, that ranking reflects both the severity of the restrictions and the scale of the suffering they produce — multiplied across nearly 100 million people.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #15
- **Estimated Christian Population:** 38–97 million (wide range reflects measurement difficulty)
- **Primary Threats:** State registration requirements, surveillance, cross removals, church demolitions, imprisonment of pastors, Sinicization policy, Bible distribution restrictions
- **Legal Status:** Christianity tolerated only within state-approved TSPM/CPCA framework; unregistered practice increasingly criminalized
- **Notable:** Pastor Wang Yi (Early Rain Covenant Church) sentenced to 9 years; hundreds of pastors imprisoned; thousands of churches closed or demolished since 2014

TITLE: Colombia — Christianity & Persecution Profile
SLUG: country-profiles/colombia
META DESCRIPTION: Colombia is a deeply Christian nation where believers in rural conflict zones face a specific and lethal threat: guerrilla and criminal organizations that target pastors, missionaries, and church leaders who challenge their social control.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Colombia, South America, FARC, ELN, Persecution, Pastors Killed, Rural Church, WWL
PERSECUTION TIER: Moderate

## Overview

Colombia is approximately 90% Christian — the vast majority Catholic, with a significant and rapidly growing evangelical Protestant community. In major cities, Christians worship freely and without threat. Colombia is not a persecution country in the sense that the state restricts religious freedom. However, Colombia presents a distinctive and serious threat for Christians in rural conflict zones: the guerrilla and organized crime groups — particularly the ELN (National Liberation Army), FARC dissident factions, and criminal networks — that control significant portions of the Colombian countryside specifically target pastors, missionaries, and church leaders who challenge their authority, refuse to cooperate with their economic activities, or provide moral community leadership that competes with their control.

## Christianity in Colombia

Spain established Catholicism as the foundational religion of Colombia through three centuries of colonial rule. The Catholic Church was deeply intertwined with political and social power through independence and well into the 20th century. A 1991 constitution formally established religious pluralism and ended the Catholic Church’s privileged constitutional status — a significant and, for minority groups, liberating change. Since then, evangelical Protestantism — particularly Pentecostal movements — has grown dramatically, especially in poor urban and rural communities. Today, Colombia’s evangelical population is estimated at 15–20% and growing.

## Armed Groups and Targeted Violence

The specific threat to Colombian Christians comes from armed non-state actors. Colombia’s half-century guerrilla conflict produced a complex landscape of Marxist insurgencies (FARC, ELN), right-wing paramilitaries, and narco-trafficking organizations. The 2016 peace agreement between the Colombian government and the main FARC organization was significant, but FARC dissident groups — those who rejected the peace agreement — continued and in some areas expanded their operations. The ELN never signed the peace agreement.

For rural churches and their pastors, these groups are a lethal threat. Pastors in conflict zones who counsel their communities against cooperation with armed groups, who provide sanctuary to displaced families, who preach against the narco-trafficking economy, or who simply build the kind of trusted community infrastructure that competes with armed group authority — these pastors are identified as threats and targeted. The threat takes various forms: extortion demands, death threats, forced displacement, and assassination. In some areas, pastors who flee leave their congregations leaderless and vulnerable.

Open Doors ranks Colombia in the lower half of the World Watch List specifically because of this pattern of violence against church leaders by non-state actors. International Christian Concern and other monitoring organizations document killings of pastors in Colombia as among the highest in Latin America. The violence is not anti-Christian per se — it is anti-social-authority, directed at any community institution that challenges armed group control. But pastors and church leaders are its primary victims precisely because of the role they play.

## Current Status

Urban Christians in Colombia’s major cities — Bogotá, Medellín, Cali — practice freely and without threat. The issue is geographically concentrated in rural conflict zones: particularly in the Pacific coast region, the Amazon borderlands, Norte de Santander, and other areas with high ELN or FARC dissident presence. In these areas, pastors are regularly killed, threatened, or forced to flee. Colombia merits monitoring and prayer specifically for these frontline rural church communities.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~90%
- **Primary Threat:** ELN, FARC dissidents, and criminal organizations targeting rural pastors and church leaders
- **Current Status:** Urban Christians fully free; rural church leaders in conflict zones at serious risk
- **Open Doors WWL:** Ranked (lower-mid tier) — specifically for non-state actor violence against church leaders
- **Notable:** Among the highest rates of pastor killings in Latin America

Proselytism is illegal, conversion from Islam carries criminal penalties, and the few known Christians face severe social and legal consequences. It is among the most restrictive environments for Christianity in the world.
CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: Comoros, Indian Ocean, Islam, Apostasy, Conversion Illegal, WWL Top 10
PERSECUTION TIER: High
WWL RANKING: #5 (Open Doors 2024)

## Overview

The Comoros — a small archipelago nation of approximately 850,000 people in the Indian Ocean between Madagascar and the East African coast — is one of the most Islamically uniform countries on earth. Approximately 98% of the population is Sunni Muslim. Islam is enshrined in the constitution as the state religion, and the legal system integrates Sharia principles alongside civil law. There are effectively no indigenous Comorian Christians — the handful of known believers are isolated individuals who practice in complete secrecy, at serious personal risk. Proselytism is illegal. Conversion from Islam is a criminal offense. Foreigners who attempt Christian ministry are expelled. The Comoros is one of the most restrictive nations in the world for Christianity, and its ranking in the Open Doors top ten reflects the near-total exclusion of the faith from public and private life.

## Historical Background

The Comoros were settled by Bantu-speaking Africans, Arab traders, and later Malagasy and Persian migrants across a long period of pre-colonial history. Islam arrived through Arab merchant networks in the early medieval period and took deep root as the cultural and religious foundation of Comorian identity. French colonization (from the mid-19th century through 1975) introduced administrative Christianity — French Catholic officials and their families — but no significant indigenous Christianization occurred. The French presence was administrative and mercantile, not missionary in any sustained sense directed at the population.

When the Comoros declared independence in 1975 — in a unilateral declaration not recognized by France for Mayotte, which voted to remain French and whose population is majority Christian today — the new state confirmed Islam as the foundation of national life. Mayotte’s Christian majority and its ongoing status as a French territory is a historical and geographic anomaly that highlights the sharpness of the religious boundary in this archipelago.

## Legal and Social Framework for Non-Muslims

The Comorian Penal Code criminalizes the exercise of any act of worship contrary to Islam with penalties including up to five years in prison. Proselytism directed at Muslims is illegal and subject to imprisonment and deportation. Importation of non-Islamic religious literature — including Bibles — is banned. Foreigners who are discovered conducting Christian activity are expelled.

For a Comorian national who converts to Christianity, the consequences are severe: family rejection, social exclusion, loss of employment, potential legal jeopardy, and physical danger. Converts cannot marry, bury their dead, or conduct normal social life within their community. The social enforcement of Islamic identity is total in a way that formal legal penalties alone cannot capture.

## The Church in Comoros

There is no organized Christian church in the Comoros. The only regular Christian worship occurs among the small number of expatriate workers — primarily from other African countries, Europe, and Asia — who are permitted to worship privately within their homes or residential compounds. They cannot build churches, hold public services, or engage in any form of outreach.

Indigenous Comorian Christians — estimated to number in the dozens, possibly low hundreds — are entirely secret. They have no community structures, no pastoral support from visible churches, and no legal recourse if they face persecution. They are among the most isolated and vulnerable believers in the world.

## Mayotte: A Different Reality

The island of Mayotte, though geographically part of the Comorian archipelago and claimed by the Comoros, has been a French territory since 1975 and voted to become a French department in 2011. Its population — largely Malagasy and African — is predominantly Catholic. The Catholic Church operates freely in Mayotte. The sharp contrast between Mayotte and the three Comoran islands reflects how geopolitical status and colonial legacy can create dramatically different religious freedom environments within a few miles of each other.

## Current Status

The Comoros ranked #5 on the 2024 Open Doors World Watch List — higher than many countries that experience active physical violence against Christians. The ranking reflects the near-total absence of any legal space for Christianity and the severity of consequences for those who pursue faith in private. There are no recent reports of mass arrests or executions of Christians — primarily because there is almost no detectable Christian presence to persecute. The situation is not one of dramatic visible violence; it is one of near-complete exclusion.

## Key Facts

- **Open Doors WWL Ranking (2024):** #5
- **Estimated Christian Population:** Fewer than 1,000 (almost all expatriate; indigenous believers number in dozens)
- **Primary Threats:** Apostasy and proselytism criminalized; Bible import banned; total social exclusion for converts
- **Legal Status:** Christianity criminalized for those who proselytize or convert from Islam; foreigners worshiping privately tolerated but at risk
- **Churches:** Zero; no public Christian worship of any kind

CATEGORY: Country Profiles
TAGS: DRC, Congo, Central Africa, ADF, Allied Democratic Forces, Church Attacks, Armed Groups, M23, WWL

## Overview

The Democratic Republic of Congo is extraordinary in its Christian identity and its suffering. Approximately 95% of the DRC’s population of nearly 100 million people identifies as Christian — among the highest Christian percentages of any large nation on earth. The Catholic Church, Protestant denominations, and the Kimbanguist Church (an indigenous African Christian movement founded in the DRC) have deep institutional roots and enormous social influence. In most of the vast country, Christians worship freely. But the DRC’s eastern provinces — North Kivu, South Kivu, Ituri, and Maniema — have been in a state of armed conflict for three decades, and Christians in those regions face violence from a complex web of armed groups, most notably the Allied Democratic Forces (ADF), which has conducted one of the continent’s most sustained campaigns of anti-Christian terror.

## Christianity in the DRC

Belgian colonization established Catholicism as the dominant religious and educational framework in the Congo from the late 19th century through independence in 1960. Catholic missions built the country’s first schools and hospitals; the church became foundational to Congolese social infrastructure in ways that have persisted long after independence. Protestant missions — Baptist, Methodist, Presbyterian, and later Pentecostal and charismatic — also gained significant footing. The Kimbanguist Church, founded by Simon Kimbangu in 1921 after a revival movement the Belgian colonial authorities tried to suppress, became one of Africa’s most significant indigenous Christian movements and is now a member of the World Council of Churches.

Since independence, Christianity has deepened its hold. The DRC is a country where churches are among the strongest civil institutions — often stronger than the state — and where faith is a lived daily reality for the majority of the population.

## The Eastern Congo Crisis and the ADF

The eastern DRC has been in near-continuous conflict since the mid-1990s, following the aftermath of the Rwandan genocide, which sent millions of refugees — including armed Hutu forces — into eastern Congo and triggered a spiral of violence involving Rwandan, Ugandan, and Congolese factions. The conflict has never fully resolved; it involves more than 100 armed groups active in eastern Congo, competing for territory, resources, and political power.

Among these groups, the Allied Democratic Forces (ADF) stand out for their specifically anti-Christian violence. The ADF was founded in Uganda in the early 1990s as an Islamist insurgency. Driven out of Uganda by the Ugandan military, it relocated to the jungles of eastern DRC near the Rwenzori Mountains and has operated there for over two decades. The ADF has carried out massacres of civilians for years. Beginning approximately 2019, however, the nature and scale of their anti-Christian violence intensified dramatically — a shift that monitoring organizations connect to the ADF’s formal affiliation with the Islamic State (declared in 2019) and to IS direction toward targeting Christian communities.

Since 2019, the ADF has burned churches, beheaded pastors and priests, and massacred Christian congregants in attacks that follow a recognizable pattern: entering villages, killing men and boys who cannot escape, burning church buildings and Bibles, and leaving markers of Islamic State affiliation. Open Doors has documented massacres of Christians at church services and in their homes across dozens of ADF attacks. The UN Group of Experts and human rights organizations have documented hundreds of deaths specifically in church-related attacks.

The M23 rebel group — backed by Rwanda — and other armed factions are also active in eastern Congo and contribute to civilian displacement and violence, including violence affecting Christians. The eastern DRC humanitarian crisis is among the world’s largest: an estimated 7 million people are internally displaced, making it the largest displacement crisis in Africa.

## The Church’s Response

Despite the violence, the Congolese church has not retreated. Catholic priests, Protestant pastors, and church lay leaders have been among the most visible and courageous advocates for peace in the eastern Congo. The Catholic Bishop of Goma and other church leaders have confronted armed groups publicly, sheltered displaced civilians in church compounds, and provided testimony to international bodies documenting atrocities. Several priests and pastors have been killed specifically because of this advocacy.

Church-based humanitarian organizations — Caritas Congo, Protestant medical and educational networks — provide the majority of healthcare and education in many parts of the east where the state has no effective presence.

## Current Status

The DRC sits in the Open Doors mid-tier ranking. The primary concern is the ADF’s sustained anti-Christian campaign in the east. The M23 offensive — which escalated dramatically in early 2024 with the capture of significant territory in North Kivu, including the outskirts of Goma — has created additional humanitarian crisis. Christians in the east are caught in a multi-party conflict in which they are both victims of targeted anti-Christian violence and general civilian victims of warfare.

The vast majority of the DRC’s 95 million Christians worship freely and are not under threat. The crisis is geographically concentrated but locally catastrophic.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~95% (~90 million)
- **Primary Threat:** ADF (Allied Democratic Forces) — sustained anti-Christian massacres in eastern Congo; general armed conflict
- **Displacement:** 7 million+ internally displaced (largest in Africa)
- **Current Status:** Most of the country safe; eastern provinces in acute crisis
- **Open Doors WWL:** Mid-tier (monitored for ADF violence)

## Overview

The Republic of Congo — also called Congo-Brazzaville to distinguish it from its much larger neighbor the Democratic Republic of Congo — is a small Central African nation of approximately 6 million people. Roughly 85% of the population identifies as Christian, with the Catholic Church and various Protestant and evangelical denominations constituting the religious landscape. Christians worship freely; the state guarantees religious liberty and does not systematically restrict any Christian denomination.

## Christianity in Congo-Brazzaville

French colonial missions — particularly Catholic — established Christianity in the region from the late 19th century. The French Congo was evangelized through a combination of Catholic missionary orders and, later, Protestant missions. At independence in 1960, the Catholic Church had deep institutional roots. The country underwent a period of Marxist governance from 1969 to 1991 — the “People’s Republic of Congo” — during which religious institutions faced some restrictions and nationalization of church schools. However, as in many Marxist African states, the suppression was never as total as in Eastern Europe or China, and the church maintained significant presence throughout.

Since democratization in 1991, the Republic of Congo has maintained legal religious freedom. The Catholic Church, Protestant denominations, evangelical movements, and the Kimbanguist Church — which crossed the river from the DRC — all operate freely. There are no documented patterns of persecution.

The country’s primary challenges are political (President Denis Sassou Nguesso has ruled for most of the past four decades, with one interruption) and economic, not religious.

## Overview

Costa Rica is among the most stable and prosperous nations in Latin America, with a long democratic tradition and full legal religious freedom. Approximately 70% of the population is Christian — roughly 52% Catholic and 18% evangelical Protestant — and the country has a unique constitutional status: Catholicism is formally named the state religion in Costa Rica’s constitution, though in practice all faiths operate freely and there is no meaningful privilege attached to this status for purposes of everyday religious life.

Christianity arrived through Spanish colonization in the 16th century. Unlike neighboring countries, Costa Rica had a relatively small indigenous population and developed primarily as a settler colony with deeply rooted Iberian Catholic culture. Protestant and evangelical Christianity grew significantly in the latter half of the 20th century, and Costa Rica is home to several Central American evangelical institutions and missionary training organizations.

Costa Rica is not on the Open Doors World Watch List and is not a country of concern by any measure of Christian persecution.

## Overview

Côte d’Ivoire is a West African nation of approximately 27 million people, roughly divided between a predominantly Christian south and a predominantly Muslim north — with a significant population following traditional religions throughout. The country endured a devastating political and military crisis between 2002 and 2011 that had religious dimensions, though the conflict was primarily ethnic and political rather than sectarian. The country has substantially recovered, and Christians in the south practice freely. An emerging threat from Sahelian jihadist groups expanding southward from Burkina Faso and Mali is a serious concern for the future.

## Christianity in Côte d’Ivoire

French colonial missionaries — Catholic — established Christianity in the coastal south beginning in the late 19th century. The south’s predominantly Akan and Bété populations largely adopted Christianity; the northern Mandé and Sénoufo populations were more influenced by trans-Saharan Islam. At independence in 1960, Côte d’Ivoire’s long-serving President Félix Houphouët-Boigny — himself a Catholic — built the country’s relative stability partly on a policy of deliberate interreligious harmony, presenting Christianity and Islam as equally legitimate expressions of Ivorian identity.

Houphouët-Boigny built the Basilica of Our Lady of Peace in Yamoussoukro — his hometown — in the late 1980s. With a seating capacity of 18,000 and a total capacity of 300,000, it is one of the largest churches in the world, a statement of extraordinary ambition — and of a Catholic president’s desire to leave a visible Christian legacy.

## The Civil War and Religious Dimensions

The civil war that began in 2002 was triggered by a military coup and revolved primarily around ethnic and political tensions — particularly the contested concept of “Ivoirité” (authentic Ivorian identity) and the exclusion of northerners from political life. The rebel movement that held the north was predominantly Muslim; the government holding the south was led by a Christian president. The war was not a religious war, but religious identity became a social marker of political allegiance, and churches in some northern areas were damaged. Post-war reconciliation explicitly addressed the need to prevent the conflict from hardening along religious lines.

## Jihadist Spillover

Since approximately 2020, jihadist groups from Burkina Faso — primarily JNIM — have conducted attacks in northern Côte d’Ivoire near the Burkinabé border. These attacks have been primarily against security forces, not Christians specifically, but the pattern of jihadist expansion into the region raises serious concerns about replication of the Burkinabé model of anti-Christian violence. The Ivorian government has responded with military operations in the border region.

## Current Status

Côte d’Ivoire is not on the Open Doors World Watch List. Christians practice freely throughout the country. Interreligious relations are generally positive, with a tradition of shared social life between Christians and Muslims. The primary concern is the potential expansion of Sahelian jihadism into northern areas — a threat that has not yet materialized at scale but warrants close monitoring.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~45% (south-concentrated)
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no systematic persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Emerging Concern:** Jihadist spillover from Burkina Faso into northern border areas

## Overview

Croatia is approximately 86% Roman Catholic and among the most overtly Catholic nations in Europe. Catholicism is woven into Croatian national identity in a way that blurs the line between religious and ethnic-national belonging. The country has full constitutional religious freedom; all denominations operate without interference. Croatia is not on the Open Doors World Watch List and is not a country of concern for Christian persecution.

## Christianity in Croatia

The Croats were Christianized in the 7th–9th centuries, and their association with Rome — rather than Constantinople — became a defining marker distinguishing them from Orthodox Serbs, their Slavic neighbors to the east and south. This Catholic-Orthodox divide was a significant fault line in the violent dissolution of Yugoslavia in the 1990s, when Croatian Catholic and Serbian Orthodox communities were on opposing sides of a devastating war. The war was primarily ethnic and nationalist rather than religious, but religious identity served as a primary social marker.

Since independence and European Union membership (2013), Croatia has been a stable democratic state. The Catholic Church retains significant cultural influence; Catholic holidays are public holidays, and religious education is common in schools. Minority religious communities — Orthodox Serbs, Muslims in Slavonia, small Protestant communities — operate legally without persecution.

## Overview

Cuba is a communist state governed by the Cuban Communist Party, which has maintained power since Fidel Castro’s 1959 revolution. The relationship between the Cuban state and the church has been one of the most consequential in the Western Hemisphere — marked by decades of suppression, uneasy accommodation, and periodic crackdowns. Christianity has survived, and by some measures grown, under communist rule. But the Cuban church — particularly its Protestant and evangelical wing — pays a significant price for that survival. Independent churches that operate outside state-approved structures are surveilled, harassed, and shut down. Pastors who speak publicly about injustice or who connect with the democracy movement face imprisonment. Since the July 2021 protests — the largest public demonstrations in Cuba in decades — the government has intensified pressure on churches that had connections to the protest movement, and imprisoned at least a dozen Christian leaders.

## Historical Background

Cuba’s colonial Catholic heritage was deep — the island was under Spanish rule from 1492 through 1898, and the Catholic Church was foundational to colonial social life. Protestant missions entered Cuba after American involvement in the Spanish-American War, and by the mid-20th century, Baptist, Methodist, and other Protestant communities had established significant work. The 1959 revolution brought immediate conflict with the church: the government nationalized Catholic schools, expelled hundreds of foreign priests and sisters, and created a hostile climate for religious practice. An estimated 135 Catholic clergy were expelled in one mass expulsion in 1961.

The government’s official position shifted from open hostility to managed tolerance over the decades. The Cuban constitution’s formal prohibition on religious belief as a condition for Communist Party membership was removed in 1992. Pope John Paul II’s visit in 1998 was a watershed moment: it opened a new phase of engagement between the Catholic Church and the Cuban state, resulting in slightly more freedom for the church in exchange for a posture of non-confrontation on political questions.

## The Protestant and Evangelical Church

Evangelical and Pentecostal Christianity in Cuba has grown significantly, particularly since the 1990s — fueled by the economic crisis of the “Special Period” following Soviet collapse, which left many Cubans spiritually searching. The house church movement expanded. Church buildings that couldn’t be officially registered multiplied informally. Prayer groups became congregations; congregations became networks.

The Cuban government manages legal Christianity through registered institutions — the Catholic Church, recognized Protestant denominations, and an official council of churches. Unregistered churches operate illegally and at risk. Registration is used as a tool of control: churches that receive it agree to restrictions on their activities; those that don’t operate in a gray zone where they can be shut down at any time.

## The 2021 Crackdown

Cuba’s July 11–12, 2021 protests — sparked by economic hardship, food and medicine shortages, and COVID-19 frustration — were the largest demonstrations since the early revolutionary period. The government’s response was brutal: mass arrests, long sentences for demonstrators, and systematic targeting of anyone associated with the protests.

Several pastors and Christian leaders were among those arrested. Pastor Lorenzo Rosales Fajardo of Palmarito de Cauto was sentenced to eight years in prison for participating in protests. Multiple house church leaders and evangelical pastors were arrested on charges of “public disorder,” “contempt of authority,” or “enemy propaganda.” The Cuban Prisoners Defenders organization documented the systematic nature of the crackdown on religious voices connected to the protest movement.

The broader context: churches in Cuba that operate independently of the state-sanctioned framework and that preach on themes of justice, dignity, or human rights are treated as political threats. The government does not always distinguish between “religious” and “political” activity when the church’s values challenge the state’s authority.

## Church Buildings and Registration

Cuba’s government has a policy of refusing to authorize new church construction in most instances. House churches that attract attention — due to growth or visibility — are ordered to close or reduce their activities. Churches that operate in unregistered spaces face fines and potential demolition of their facilities. Some congregations have had buildings seized or demolished on pretextual grounds.

Open Doors ranks Cuba in the mid-tier of the World Watch List. The primary persecution is state-directed, targeted at independent church leadership, and delivered through legal mechanisms — imprisonment on political charges, registration denial, building demolition — rather than primarily through physical violence, though that too has occurred. The Cuban church endures, grows quietly, and pays a real price for doing so.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~60% (Catholic and Protestant/evangelical combined; definitions of practice vary widely)
- **Primary Threats:** State surveillance, registration denial for independent churches, imprisonment of pastors connected to democracy movement, church building restrictions
- **Current Status:** State-controlled Christianity tolerated; independent churches under growing pressure; significant crackdown since 2021
- **Open Doors WWL:** Mid-tier ranked
- **Notable:** Pastor Lorenzo Rosales Fajardo sentenced to 8 years following 2021 protests; dozens of Christian leaders imprisoned

## Overview

Cyprus has been politically divided since 1974, when Turkey invaded the northern third of the island following a coup supported by the Greek military junta. The internationally recognized Republic of Cyprus — controlling the southern two-thirds — is approximately 78% Greek Cypriot, predominantly Eastern Orthodox. The Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus, recognized only by Turkey, is majority Muslim. Christians in the Republic of Cyprus worship entirely freely. In the north, the historic issue is not active persecution of Christians but restricted access to Orthodox churches, monasteries, and Christian heritage sites — some of which have been converted to mosques or allowed to deteriorate.

## Christianity in Cyprus

Christianity on Cyprus has deep apostolic roots: the Apostle Paul and Barnabas (a Cypriot himself) preached on the island during the first missionary journey recorded in Acts 13. The Church of Cyprus — one of the autocephalous churches of Eastern Orthodoxy — traces its origin to this apostolic foundation and received formal recognition of its independence at the Council of Ephesus in 431 AD, making it one of the oldest independent churches in the world.

The Crusader period (12th–14th centuries) brought Frankish Catholic rule and imposed Catholicism on the island, subordinating the Orthodox hierarchy — a humiliation still remembered. Ottoman conquest in 1571 brought Islam and restored the Orthodox hierarchy in a complicated relationship with the new Muslim rulers. British colonial rule (1878–1960) introduced political modernity and eventual independence.

## The Divided Island and Heritage Sites

The 1974 division displaced approximately 200,000 Greek Cypriots from the north, where they had lived for centuries. The historic churches, monasteries, and cemeteries they left behind have faced varying fates under Turkish administration: some have been converted to mosques (including the Cathedral of St. Sophia in Nicosia, now Selimiye Mosque); others have become museums; others have deteriorated through neglect. The icons and artifacts from many northern churches were removed and sold on the international art market — an act of cultural looting documented in extensive legal proceedings.

The Cyprus issue remains a frozen conflict. The Republic of Cyprus is a member of the European Union; the north is under Turkish administration. Christians in the Republic of Cyprus worship entirely freely and face no persecution. The Christian heritage of the north is a genuine cultural and religious rights concern, though it does not constitute active persecution of a Christian community.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~78% (in the Republic of Cyprus; north is Muslim-majority)
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom in the Republic of Cyprus; no active persecution
- **Heritage Concern:** Historic Orthodox churches in northern Cyprus converted, deteriorated, or stripped of artifacts
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked

## Overview

Czechia — the Czech Republic — is one of the most secular countries on earth. Only about 14% of Czechs identify as Christian by active practice, and roughly 65–70% of the population identifies as non-religious. Christians face no persecution whatsoever; the country has full religious freedom. Czechia’s extraordinary secularism is itself a consequence of Christian history — specifically the trauma of forced re-Catholicization after the defeat of the Protestant Bohemian forces at the Battle of White Mountain in 1620, and the subsequent association of the Catholic Church with Habsburg oppression, which generated an anti-clerical tradition that deepened further under communist rule.

## A Complicated Christian History

Bohemia and Moravia — the historical regions composing Czechia — were at the center of the European Reformation before the Reformation. Jan Hus, rector of Charles University in Prague, preached church reform a full century before Luther, was condemned at the Council of Constance, and was burned at the stake in 1415. The Hussite movement — which emerged in his wake — anticipated many Protestant themes and produced a remarkable tradition of vernacular scripture and democratic church governance. When the full Protestant Reformation arrived in the 16th century, Bohemia became one of its strongest centers; the Letter of Majesty (1609) guaranteed religious freedom to Bohemian Protestants.

The Battle of White Mountain in 1620, in which Catholic Habsburg forces decisively defeated the Protestant Bohemian uprising, ended this period. Mass exile of Protestant nobility and clergy followed; forced re-Catholicization was imposed by the state; the Jesuit order supervised the dismantling of Protestant institutions. This trauma was formative: Czechs associated Catholicism with foreign domination, and the seeds of deep anti-clericalism were planted.

The communist period (1948–1989) further suppressed religious life, closed seminaries, imprisoned clergy, and promoted atheism. By the time communism fell, Czech Christianity had been weakened by centuries of forced association with power — first Habsburg Catholic power, then communist anti-religious power — and the result is a population that has largely disengaged from institutional religion entirely.

## Current Status

Czech Christians — Catholic, Protestant, and others — worship freely. The Catholic Church is rebuilding its public presence after decades of communism. Small evangelical and charismatic communities are active. There is no state persecution and no social pressure against Christians. The challenge for the Czech church is not persecution but indifference — ministering in a culture where religion is widely regarded as irrelevant or associated with historical oppression.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Active Christian Population:** ~14%
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Historical Note:** Jan Hus (burned 1415) and the Hussite movement anticipated the Protestant Reformation by a century; White Mountain trauma (1620) deeply shaped Czech secularism

## Overview

Denmark is a Nordic welfare state with full religious freedom and a historically Lutheran Christian heritage. The Church of Denmark (Folkekirken) is the official state church — supported by a church tax and formally linked to the Danish state — but all denominations and religions operate freely, and membership in the state church is voluntary. Approximately 72% of Danes are members of the Church of Denmark, though active weekly attendance is far lower — Denmark is one of the most secular societies in the world by practice, even while most Danes retain a nominal connection to the national church.

Christianity arrived in Denmark through missionary activity in the 9th and 10th centuries, particularly through the work of Ansgar of Bremen (“Apostle of the North”) and culminating in the baptism of King Harald Bluetooth around 960 AD. The Reformation reached Denmark in 1536 when King Christian III established Lutheranism as the state religion, an arrangement that has persisted in some form ever since.

Danish Christians of all backgrounds — Lutheran, Catholic, Baptist, Pentecostal, and others — practice without restriction. Denmark is not on the Open Doors World Watch List and has no pattern of Christian persecution.

## Overview

Djibouti is a small country of approximately one million people at the strategic junction of the Red Sea and the Gulf of Aden, bordering Somalia, Ethiopia, and Eritrea. It is 94% Muslim. Christians represent approximately 6% of the population — a figure composed almost entirely of expatriate workers, Ethiopian and Eritrean immigrants, and a tiny number of indigenous Djiboutian converts who practice in extreme secrecy. Islam is the state religion under the constitution; sharia informs aspects of family law; and proselytism directed at Muslims is illegal. The country is not violently anti-Christian — it is, by the standards of the Horn of Africa region, relatively stable — but the environment for indigenous Christian faith is deeply restrictive.

## Christianity in Djibouti

There is no ancient tradition of Christianity in Djibouti. The territory was populated by the Afar and Issa Somali peoples, both Muslim, long before French colonization established Djibouti as French Somaliland in the late 19th century. French administrators and their families worshiped in the small Catholic church built for them in Djibouti City; this remains essentially the character of the Catholic presence today — a small church serving expatriates and immigrants with no significant indigenous Djiboutian congregation. Protestant and evangelical missions have similarly worked primarily among the foreign-born population.

Ethiopian and Eritrean immigrants — many of whom are Christian, belonging to the Ethiopian Orthodox or Eritrean Orthodox traditions — form the largest indigenous-African Christian presence in Djibouti. They can worship in their own communities and maintain their own religious practices.

## Converts and Restrictions

A Djiboutian who converts from Islam to Christianity faces severe social consequences. Family rejection, loss of employment, community exclusion, and physical danger from family members are all documented risks. The legal framework does not explicitly criminalize conversion, but social enforcement is powerful, and state institutions provide no protection for converts. Pastors who disciple converts from Muslim backgrounds face threats.

Foreign Christians working in Djibouti must be careful. Worship within established expatriate communities is permitted; organized outreach to Djiboutian Muslims is not. The law against proselytism carries penalties including imprisonment and deportation.

## Strategic Importance and International Presence

Djibouti hosts foreign military bases from the United States, France, China, Japan, Italy, and others — the country’s strategic location has made it valuable to global powers. This international military presence includes significant numbers of Christian service members who worship in chapels on their bases. This expatriate-military Christian presence does not affect the situation for indigenous Djiboutian Christians.

## Current Status

Djibouti is not in the top tier of the Open Doors World Watch List but is monitored. The situation for the small number of indigenous Djiboutian Christians is one of social isolation and secrecy rather than dramatic state violence. The expatriate Christian community worships without interference within defined limits. The overall environment is restrictive but not acutely violent.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~6% (almost entirely expatriate and immigrant)
- **Primary Restrictions:** Proselytism illegal; converts from Islam face severe social consequences; no indigenous church community
- **Current Status:** Private worship permitted for foreigners; indigenous Christians practice in extreme secrecy
- **Open Doors WWL:** Monitored; not in top tier

## Overview

The Dominican Republic is a majority-Christian nation — approximately 68% Catholic and 18% Protestant (largely Pentecostal and evangelical) — occupying the eastern two-thirds of the island of Hispaniola. It has full religious freedom under its constitution, and Christians of all denominations practice without legal restriction or systematic social persecution.

Columbus’s first permanent settlement in the Americas was established on Hispaniola, and the Catholic Church arrived with the earliest Spanish colonists. The island was the base of Catholic missionary expansion throughout the Americas in the 16th century; the first cathedral in the Americas was built in Santo Domingo (completed 1541) and is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site. That deep colonial Catholic heritage remains visible in Dominican culture, public life, and family practice, even as evangelical Pentecostalism has grown substantially over the past several decades.

The Dominican Republic is not on the Open Doors World Watch List and is not a country of concern for Christian persecution. The country’s challenges — economic inequality, migration pressures, political instability — do not include religious persecution.

## Overview

Ecuador is approximately 80% Christian — with a Catholic majority and a growing evangelical minority — and has full constitutional religious freedom. Christians face no systematic persecution in Ecuador; all denominations operate freely, including within indigenous communities where Christianity has blended in complex ways with Andean spiritual traditions.

Spanish colonization brought Catholicism to Ecuador in the 16th century. The Jesuit, Dominican, and Franciscan orders established missions throughout the country, including significant work among Andean indigenous communities and the Amazonian peoples of the Oriente. The 19th-century liberal revolution reduced the church’s political power, but Catholic cultural influence remained deep. Protestant and evangelical missions intensified from the mid-20th century; the martyrdom of five American missionaries — Jim Elliot, Nate Saint, Roger Youderian, Ed McCully, and Pete Fleming — who were killed while attempting to contact the Huaorani (Auca) people in 1956 became one of the most well-known events in modern Protestant mission history. The subsequent peaceful contact made by their widows and the eventual widespread conversion of the Huaorani people is among the most remarkable mission stories of the 20th century.

Ecuador is not on the Open Doors World Watch List.

## Key Facts

- **Estimated Christian Population:** ~80%
- **Current Status:** Full religious freedom; no persecution
- **Open Doors WWL:** Not ranked
- **Notable:** 1956 martyrdom of Jim Elliot and four fellow missionaries attempting to reach the Huaorani; subsequent peaceful mission became one of the defining stories of 20th-century evangelical missions
